# Peer Review — Reviewer #3 (Canadian political-science perspective)

**Manuscript:** "Alberta Electoral Boundaries Audit — Academic and Legal Edition" (`report_academic.md`, April 2026 draft).
**Reviewer role:** Senior Canadian political-science scholar in the Carty–Pal–Courtney lineage.
**Recommended venue for routing question:** see "Reviewer's assessment of scope and venue fit" at the end.

---

## Summary of manuscript

The author (a final-year Mount Royal University undergraduate) conducts a reproducible, falsifiability-gated forensic audit of Alberta's 2025–26 Electoral Boundaries Commission, comparing the three-member majority and two-member minority 89-seat maps against the 2019 baseline across six dimensions: population equality (A1–A3), four partisan-bias metrics (EG, mean–median, seats–votes at 50/50, declination), formal packing/cracking/engineered-boundary signature detection, a 100,000-plan MCMC neutral-ensemble placement, geographic coherence, and a procedural audit of the April 16, 2026 legislative motion setting aside the majority report. The paper concludes the minority map is "directionally consistent" with a sub-threshold UCP-favouring partisan asymmetry across five of six dimensions under 2023-vote input, and frames the April 16 action as "the most government-controlled response among the three most commonly cited Canadian comparator cases" (Quebec 1992, Ontario 1996, BC 2008). The work is extensively self-red-teamed, with Monte Carlo uncertainty, cross-election stability testing, declination disagreement, a Chen–Rodden natural-packing counter-test, and a symmetry-of-test-selection audit. Statutory material (*Electoral Boundaries Commission Act*, *Reference re Saskatchewan*) is engaged; contribution is positioned as evidentiary rather than constitutional adjudication.

## Overall recommendation

**Accept with major revisions.**

Computational and statistical discipline is above the level normally seen from graduate-student first submissions, let alone undergraduate work; the falsifiability gates (G0–G5, RT1–RT7), Monte Carlo CI reporting, transparent declination disagreement, and §5.6 symmetric counter-test are all exemplary. Structural findings are reproducible and red-team discipline is visible throughout. The problem is framing, not evidence: the paper reads as a US-literature application with a light Canadian veneer. §2 and §5.9 cite Courtney (2001) once without substantive engagement, do not cite Pal at all, cite Carty only for *Big Tent Politics* (on the federal Liberal Party, not boundary commissions), and omit Wesley from the References entirely. Rewrite §2, §5.9.3, and §6 with serious Canadian-lineage engagement and correct the §5.9.3 factual errors about the comparator cases, and this paper can move to "Accept with minor revisions" in a second round.

## Major comments

1. **The comparator trio in §5.9.3 is materially mis-described and the "most government-controlled" claim is consequently overstated.** All three comparators need repair.

   - **Quebec 1992** was not "narrow amendments to commission report via National Assembly legislation" as the paper implies. The 1992 decree (1992 GOQ 2, 3197) was the *publication* of the Commission de la représentation électorale's delimitation of 125 divisions under Quebec's *Act respecting electoral representation*, which fixes the chamber between 122 and 125. Better understood as a commission-driven cycle within a statutory envelope, not a government override. A more relevant Quebec comparator is the 2011 Bill 132 controversy, where the National Assembly refused to proclaim the CRE's delimitation — much closer to the Alberta 2026 motion.
   - **Ontario 1996 (Bill 81, Fewer Politicians Act)** was not a provincial boundary-commission override at all. Ontario had no standing provincial commission in 1996 — Bill 81 simply *adopted* federal-commission boundaries (reducing from 130 to 103 MPPs). Legitimate Ontario comparators are the 2005 *Representation Act* (re-adding 4 northern seats) and the 2015 *Representation Statute Law Amendment Act* (adding 15 seats). Fewer Politicians is a "subordinate us to Elections Canada" story, not an override.
   - **BC 2008** is described as legislating "to retain more Northern seats than the commission recommended." Incomplete. The 2007 preliminary report proposed 81 seats; public and partisan pressure led the commission to produce *two* alternative reports in February 2008, and the Legislature then passed the *Electoral Districts Act* (SBC 2008, c 14) adopting the 85-seat "alternative" map **drafted by the commission itself** as a contingency. This is government amendment to commission output, not replacement of the drafting process; the commission remained in control.

   The "most government-controlled among the three most commonly cited comparators" language should be rewritten. A stronger and actually-accurate framing: "The April 16, 2026 motion replaces the commission's drafting process with a legislature-selected committee, a departure from any Canadian provincial boundary-adjustment cycle reviewed in Courtney (2001) and subsequent scholarship." The paper can sustain that claim; the three-comparator rhetorical device cannot.

2. **Pal is absent, Courtney under-cited, Carty miscited, Wesley missing as scholar.** The Canadian literature review in §2 is thin. Michael Pal's *Fractured Right to Vote* (McGill Law Journal 61:2, 2016) is the single most directly relevant peer-reviewed Canadian article for this topic — Pal addresses exactly the discretion/design-choice nexus the audit operationalises. Pal and Choudhry, "Constituency Boundaries in Canada" (in Aucoin, Jarvis, & Turnbull eds., *Democratizing the Constitution*, 2011), and Pal and Choudhry, "Still Not Equal? Visible Minority Vote Dilution in Canada" (*Canadian Political Science Review* 8:1, 2014), are also directly relevant. Carty is cited only to *Big Tent Politics* (2015, a book on the federal Liberal Party); the redistribution-relevant Carty work is his service on the 2002–03 and 2022–23 federal BC electoral boundaries commissions and his IRPP policy papers. Jared Wesley — quoted in the paper as a commentator — is a University of Alberta political scientist with peer-reviewed work on Alberta party politics and is himself a member of the 2025–26 commission on whose output the paper reports. He should be cited as a scholar (*Code Politics*, UBC Press, 2011; edited *Big Worlds*, U. Toronto Press, 2015), not only as a newspaper source. Courtney (2001) is acknowledged as "the authoritative scholarly treatment" but the paper does not engage Courtney's five-principles framework (county-line preservation, party-leader-seat protection, growth-area additions, urban-vs-rural population differentials, rep-by-pop) — a natural frame for §5.8 and §5.9. Add at least: Pal (2016), Pal & Choudhry (2011), Wesley (2011 and post-2015 Alberta-politics work), Courtney (2001) substantively, and a secondary reference to Seidle's Royal Commission on Electoral Reform (1991) work on the institutional genealogy of Canadian commission independence.

3. **"Gerrymander" vocabulary is imported from a different institutional world and the paper partially but not fully marks the distinction.** This is the most important comment of this review. The US detection vocabulary (packing, cracking, engineered boundary, efficiency gap, mean-median, declination) is developed within a regime in which partisan intent is *built into* the drawing process: US state legislatures draw maps with acknowledged partisan objectives, and the statistical machinery is designed to detect when that partisan activity exceeds normative or constitutional bounds. Canadian provincial commissions draw under statutory non-partisan mandates; the *Electoral Boundaries Commission Act* (RSA 2000, c E-3) in Alberta excludes partisan intent *as a matter of statute*. A UCP-favourable Alberta map is therefore a *structural artefact* or a *commissioner-level preference drift*, not a partisan-drawing output in the sense that a Texas or Wisconsin map is. The paper handles this partially well (§5.2.5 acknowledges Chen–Rodden natural packing; §1 notes the 90% directional confidence rather than claiming statistical significance; §4.5 clearly scopes what the audit does not claim) but the overall vocabulary still imports the US frame in ways that a Canadian reviewer will flag. The title uses "Audit"; the subtitle speaks of a "forensic assessment" — Canadian-commission scholarship would use "redistribution review" or "commission-output assessment." The three signature types (P1–P3 packing; C1–C3 cracking; E1–E3 engineered boundary) are directly transplanted. I would not ask the author to abandon the vocabulary — the statistical metrics are the right tools regardless of regime — but I would ask for a stand-alone "Institutional Context" paragraph in §2 that names the non-partisan-mandate difference explicitly and explains *why* the same metrics in two institutional frames carry different inferential weight. A UCP-favouring EG under a Canadian commission is evidence of something different than a Republican-favouring EG under a Texas legislature, and the paper currently lets the reader collapse those.

4. **The "71st percentile" claim on the Canadian redistribution base rate (§5.2.1) rests on n=7 cycles with a single-anchor compression factor and should not carry the rhetorical weight it does.** The Canadian comparative base rate at `analysis/methodology/canadian_base_rate_computed.md` is an honest and commendable attempt to contextualize Alberta 2025–26. But: the compression factor (0.455) is calibrated from the Alberta anchor itself, the sample (n=7) excludes Nova Scotia 2019, Prince Edward Island cycles, Newfoundland and Labrador 2015, New Brunswick 2016, and all federal sub-commissions except Alberta 2022, and the proxy identity holds only under the idealized two-party wasted-vote formula, not under the audit's actual urban-weight-blended attribution. A credible Canadian base rate for inter-map EG asymmetry would include at least: federal 2012 (all provinces), every provincial cycle 1996–2024 where a majority and minority or interim and final report produced differing maps, and the BC 2008 unique two-report structure (which the proxy currently assigns Δs=0 but is arguably Δs≈2 under its 85-seat "alternative" branch). Even more importantly, the "71st percentile" language implies a distribution dense enough to support percentile statements; with n=7 and two non-zero observations besides the anchor, the 71st percentile is one data point from the 57th and one from the 86th. The paper should either: (a) report the rank ("Alberta 2025–26's point estimate is the second-largest of seven sampled cycles"), or (b) commit to a full base-rate build-out as a separate paper and scope the current manuscript's framing to "Alberta 2025–26 is not a median cycle; the small Canadian sample cannot support tighter inference." I strongly recommend (a) as a minor revision.

5. **Alberta 2017 is presented incorrectly and this matters for the contribution framing.** The 2017 Bielby commission kept Alberta at 87 seats (the total set by the 2010 Walter commission when expanding from 83); it did *not* produce an 89-seat map. The 2026 cycle's proposed 89-seat maps are therefore the first expansion beyond 87 in the province's history, which strengthens the *Reference re Saskatchewan* "effective representation" argument: adding seats can improve effective representation even if reallocating them partisanly compromises it. The 2019 baseline of 87 seats is correct in the paper but any reference implying 2017 changed the count needs correcting. Additionally, §5.9.2 should note this is the first Alberta commission cycle with a 3–2 split producing two incompatible maps; the 2010 and 2017 cycles each had individual-commissioner minority reports that did not redraw the map. This strengthens the "unusual procedural posture" argument rather than weakening it.

6. **Alberta's partisan volatility is not adequately acknowledged as a case-selection risk.** The cross-election stability test (§5.2.3) reports that the asymmetry direction reverses under 2019 votes, nears neutrality under 2015, and replicates only under 2023 and April 2026 polling. The paper reads this as sensitivity to "2020s-era voter distribution." A Canadian reviewer will read it as: Alberta is, by any reasonable measure, the most electorally volatile province in the federation — PCs 44 consecutive years to 2015, NDP majority 2015–19, UCP majorities 2019 and 2023, the Wildrose merger, the Republican Party of Alberta contesting byelections, the April 2026 polling showing UCP landslide conditions. A 1-seat partisan-asymmetry finding that reverses direction between 2019 and 2023 and between 2023 and April 2026 polling is more plausibly reading the noise of Alberta's partisan environment than reading the boundary maps. I would ask the author to add an Alberta-specific volatility paragraph to §5.2.3 or §6 (Discussion) that acknowledges this — see Bratt, Brown, Sayers, & Taras (2019) *Orange Chinook* and the *Canadian Political Science Review* 2020 special issue on Alberta — and weights the structural findings (§5.1, §5.8) more heavily in the synthesis because they are the dimensions robust to Alberta's electoral churn. This is not a fatal problem; it is a Canadian-context framing problem that makes the paper more defensible.

7. **The 2022 Alberta federal sub-commission is relevant and not engaged.** The Alberta 2022 federal redistribution produced 37 seats (up from 34), with a commission chaired by Justice J.D. Bruce McDonald and including Donald Barry (U. Calgary, political science emeritus) and Donna Wilson. It unified Red Deer, split Airdrie-Chestermere (creating a dedicated Airdrie–Chestermere federal seat, which intersects directly with the paper's §5.3.2 Airdrie cracking finding), and produced a unanimous final report. The paper mentions this only in passing in the base-rate catalogue. Given that the 2022 federal sub-commission addressed many of the *same* Alberta geographic-community questions (Airdrie, Red Deer, Lethbridge, Calgary suburbs) that the 2025–26 provincial commission is now addressing, its treatment choices are a direct Canadian comparator. In particular, the federal sub-commission chose a 2-district treatment of Airdrie (Airdrie–Chestermere + Banff–Airdrie, now Airdrie–Cochrane), matching the Alberta provincial majority's 2-district approach and divergent from the provincial minority's 4-district approach. This is a strong Canadian benchmark for the paper's Airdrie cracking finding and should be incorporated into §5.3.2 and §5.6.

8. **The contribution claim is ambiguous between three positionings, only one of which routes well to a Canadian venue.** §1 claims (a) a symmetric falsifiability-gated framework, (b) pre-registration against the forthcoming third map, and (c) data-provenance documentation. These are process contributions. What the paper *should also* claim, if routing to a Canadian venue, is (d) "the first systematic computational audit of an Alberta provincial electoral boundary commission's output, with attention to its divergence from Canadian commission norms." Without (d), the paper reads as "US tools applied to an Alberta case" — a methodology-import paper better suited to *Election Law Journal* or *Political Analysis* than to a Canadian venue. Commit to (d) as the headline; make (a)–(c) supporting.

## Minor comments

1. **Abstract inconsistency in dimension numbering.** The Abstract lists "six dimensions: (A), (B), (C), (D), (4), and (5)." The letter/number mix is confusing — these are the old v0.1 section labels bleeding through. Renumber to (1)–(6) or use verbal descriptors.

2. **Courtney citation should be to the substantive chapters.** §2 cites Courtney (2001) as "the authoritative scholarly treatment" without chapter pin. For the population-equality work in §5.1 cite Courtney (2001) chs. 6–7 ("The Senate Floor" / "Drawing the Boundaries") directly; for the procedural argument in §5.9 cite chs. 10–11 ("Public Hearings" / "Judicial Review"). Without pincites, the Courtney citation reads as ornamental.

3. **Charter case list.** *Figueroa v. Canada* (2003) and *Frank v. Canada* (2019) are correctly identified as context for the Charter §3 right to vote, not as boundary-drawing authorities. Consider adding *Raîche v. Canada (Attorney General)*, [2004] FC 679 (a successful Charter §3 challenge to New Brunswick federal boundaries) and *Cassista v. Canada (Attorney General)*, 2014 FC 398, both of which are in the Appendix F prose but not in the References. The Charter-law genealogy is better-served by these two Federal Court cases than by *Haig v. Canada*.

4. **"Reference re Saskatchewan" is routinely called "the Saskatchewan Reference" or "Re Saskatchewan Electoral Boundaries" in Canadian legal-academic usage.** Either form is acceptable, but stick to one.

5. **"Independent commission" vs "non-partisan commission".** The paper uses both. Canadian convention is "independent" to describe the relationship to government and "non-partisan" to describe the mandate. A quick terminology pass clarifies whether the paper means commissioner selection (independent) or drawing criteria (non-partisan) at each usage.

6. **Alberta's *Electoral Boundaries Commission Act* citation.** The paper cites "RSA 2000, c E-3" correctly but never pincites to §3, §4, §12, §13, §14, or §15 in the References block. Given the heavy reliance on §12 and §15 in the findings, the statute citation in References should be at the chapter-and-section level in the body text; currently it is bare.

7. **Declination metric reading.** §5.2.4 is transparent about B6's disagreement with B2–B4 and interprets it correctly as "different face of the same structural pattern." This is good. The one quibble: Warrington (2018) is at §5.2's test battery but the more apposite Warrington citation for the Alberta disagreement is Warrington (2019), "A comparison of partisan gerrymandering measures," *Election Law Journal* 18(3). The paper cites both; just move 2019 forward as the primary cite when discussing the disagreement.

8. **Ferejohn & Riker are not cited.** The paper uses Gelman & King (1994) and Grofman (1983) for the seats–votes-curve methodology. Ferejohn (1974) *Pork Barrel Politics* and Riker's seat-vote work on parliamentary systems are relevant for the FPTP-CA vs FPTP-US distinction noted in the prompt. If the author's claim is that seat-vote curves translate from US House applications to Canadian parliamentary applications, Ferejohn and Riker are the bridges. (Optional; only if the author wants to tighten the theoretical framing.)

9. **Wesley quotation at §"The Argument".** I cannot locate §"The Argument" in the `report_academic.md` I reviewed — the TOC goes §1 through §8 plus appendices. This may be a remnant of the pre-reorganisation draft. Check against `analysis/red_team/academic_reorganization_log.md` for stale cross-references.

10. **Statistics Canada Census usage is transparent but the Alberta Treasury Board divergence needs a sentence on *why* the commission used the 2024 TBF estimate.** The paper says the commission declared its basis as "2021 census updated to a July 1, 2024 Office of Statistics and Information estimate." A Canadian reviewer wants to know: does this choice reflect standard Alberta commission practice (yes, 2017 Bielby commission did the same), and how does it compare to other provincial commissions' statutory-basis choices (Quebec uses electors-by-register, BC uses Statistics Canada quarterly, Manitoba uses decennial census)? The paper has the material at §3.3 and `analysis/reports/plan_b_cross_check.md`; add two sentences placing Alberta in the Canadian practice spectrum.

11. **The 4,765 VA polygons carrying 52.5% of 2023 two-party votes caveat.** This is front-loaded in the author-disclosure sense but is buried in §5.4 and § E.3. The reader sees the MCMC 100,000-plan ensemble result before they see the coverage caveat. Consider moving the Advance/Mobile/Special Ballot coverage note to the §3.2 "Coverage caveats" block so it is disclosed up-front alongside the 2026 shapefile non-release.

12. **Sancton (2021) and Smith (2010) in the References block.** Sancton's *Limits of Boundaries* is cited; Smith's *Canada's Deep Crown* is cited. Neither is referenced in the body text as far as I can tell. Either use them in §2 or §5.9.3 or remove from References — unused citations are a legitimate flag for reviewer attention.

## Specific strengths

- **The falsifiability and red-team discipline is outstanding.** Gates G0–G5, the RT1–RT7 stress-test grid in §5.7, the declination disagreement read honestly in §5.2.4, the Chen–Rodden counter-test in §5.2.5, the E2 reformulation discipline correction in §5.3.3, and the public-submission-record verification in §5.9.4 all show a standard of self-auditing that most published work does not reach. The three findings that "ran against the author's prior and were retained" (§1) is a template other authors should learn from.
- **The MCMC 100,000-plan neutral-ensemble baseline in §5.4 is methodologically serious.** The ReCom 0.3.2 run, the 2019-baseline substrate choice, the full-coverage rescore at 10,000 → 100,000 samples with the 88-row crosswalk, and the honest disclosure that the effective sample size (148–160) is below MGGG "lawsuit-grade" but sufficient for policy-comparison framing — all exemplary. The structural-UCP-floor finding ("ensemble median mean-median is −0.019 and ensemble median seats-at-50/50 is 0.448") is the most important quantitative contribution of the paper and the one a Canadian commission-scholarship reader will carry away.
- **The pre-registration against the November 2026 MLA-committee map (§5.5) is a genuinely novel discipline for Canadian redistribution scholarship.** Open Science Framework submission with embargoed release — this is not something Courtney (2001) or Pal (2016) had available and the paper is, to my knowledge, the first Canadian-context deployment.
- **The symmetry-of-test-selection counter-test in §5.6 is a real methodological contribution.** The Edmonton zone-packing and the city-wide 4-way split counter-test are exactly what a Canadian reviewer would ask for if the paper did not already contain it, and the Lethbridge / Red Deer extension of the Airdrie pattern is a significant substantive finding that strengthens the paper's §5.3.2 cracking argument. Hold this counter-test up in the revision as an example of how Canadian-context auditing should be done.
- **The §5.9.4 tiered verdict on Appendix C's "no public support" claim is careful and well-calibrated.** The precisely-wrong / effectively-wrong / defensible three-tier structure resists the flattening that both sides would prefer. This is good Canadian-public-administration scholarship.

## Reviewer's assessment of scope and venue fit

The paper in its current form reads as a methodology-demonstration paper with an Alberta case study, which routes most naturally to *Political Analysis*, *Election Law Journal*, or *Statistics and Public Policy* — US-adjacent venues comfortable with the packing/cracking/efficiency-gap vocabulary and not expecting Canadian-institutional contextualisation. If the author's goal is a Canadian political-science venue, the paper needs the revision I outline in Major Comments 1, 2, 3, and 8: correct the comparator cases (Quebec 1992, Ontario 1996, BC 2008 all misframed), fill in the Canadian literature engagement (Pal, Courtney substantively, Wesley as scholar), mark the Canadian-institutional frame-divergence from US partisan-drawing, and commit to positioning (d) — "the first systematic computational audit of an Alberta commission, placed within Canadian commission norms" — as the headline contribution. With those revisions, the paper fits *Canadian Public Policy* most naturally (quantitative, policy-relevant, public-administration-adjacent), then *Canadian Parliamentary Review* for a shorter practitioner-facing version, and potentially *Canadian Political Science Review* for the full methodology-plus-case manuscript. *Canadian Journal of Political Science* is achievable but would require a deeper Canadian-literature integration than the current draft exhibits and a sharper theoretical contribution beyond "apply US tools to Canadian case"; I would not recommend CJPS for the first submission, but it becomes a plausible second-round target after the Major Comments are addressed.
