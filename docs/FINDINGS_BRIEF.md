# Alberta's Two Electoral Maps: What the Numbers Show

**A plain-language summary — May 2026**

---

## Start here: why riding boundaries matter to you

When you vote in an Alberta election, you vote in your **riding** — the geographic area you share with your MLA (Member of the Legislative Assembly). Your riding might cover your neighbourhood, your town, or a chunk of a city. Every riding elects exactly one person.

The government is run by whichever party wins the most ridings. 89 ridings total. Win 45 and you control the legislature. Win 44 and you don't.

Here is the part that matters: **somebody has to decide where the riding lines go.** Move a line a few streets over and thousands of voters shift from one riding to another. That can change who wins that riding — without a single person changing their vote. If you move enough lines in the right direction, you can change the outcome of an entire election.

That is why riding boundaries are not just a technical detail. They are, in effect, a set of choices about whose votes will count more.

---

## What happened

To prevent the government from drawing its own riding lines (which would be like letting one team write the rules of its own game), Alberta uses an independent commission — a group of five people who are supposed to draw the lines fairly. In 2025-26, that commission could not agree. Three members produced one map. Two members produced a different one.

On April 16, 2026, the provincial government threw out both commission maps and gave the job to a five-person committee of MLAs. Three of the five MLAs are from the governing United Conservative Party (UCP). The main opposition is the New Democratic Party (NDP).

This audit compared the two commission maps to ask: do they treat both parties' voters the same way?

---

## How the audit works

You cannot just look at two maps and say which one is fair. Maps are complicated. So this audit used two approaches:

**First:** It measured six specific things about each map — things like how evenly the population is spread across ridings, and how many pieces cities are cut into.

**Second:** It used a computer program to draw 1 million random Alberta maps, all following the same rules the commission had to follow. Then it asked: where do the two commission maps fall compared to all those random maps? Are they normal, or extreme?

Think of it like a height chart at a school. If you measure a thousand students and find that most are between 150 cm and 180 cm, a student who is 210 cm is unusual — not because being tall is wrong, but because it is rare. This audit does the same thing with riding maps.

---

## Finding 1: The ridings should be roughly the same size

Every riding is supposed to hold about the same number of people. The reason is simple: if your riding has 20,000 people and the riding next door has 40,000, your MLA represents half as many people. That means your vote carries twice the weight of your neighbour's. That is not fair.

Under the **majority map**, the average riding is off from the ideal size by about **3,180 people**.

Under the **minority map**, the same gap is **4,707 people — 48% worse**.

Neither map is perfect. But the minority map's riding sizes are considerably more unequal.

---

## Finding 2: At a tied vote, the maps produce very different seat counts

Here is the clearest test. Imagine the next election ended in a perfect tie: every Albertan who voted chose exactly 50% NDP and 50% UCP. No one changed their mind. No one moved. Just a perfectly split vote.

Under those conditions, how many seats would each party win?

| Map | NDP seats won | UCP seats won |
|---|---|---|
| Majority map | **48 out of 89** | 41 out of 89 |
| Minority map | **43 out of 89** | 46 out of 89 |

Same votes. Same voters. Different maps. A **5-seat difference**.

To understand what 5 seats means: you need 45 seats to control the legislature. Under the majority map at a tied vote, the NDP wins 48 — a governing majority. Under the minority map at the same tied vote, the NDP wins 43 — three seats short of a majority, with the UCP winning 46 — a governing majority for them instead.

**The same election result produces a different government, depending on which map is used.**

The computer ran 1 million random maps. Under a tied vote, the majority map's result looked normal — it sat in the middle of the pack. The minority map's result was extreme: **fewer than 100 of the 1 million random maps** produced a seat split that far in favour of the UCP.

---

## Finding 3: Some votes count less than others — and both maps are not equal here

Every election, some votes do not actually help their party win more seats. There are two ways a vote gets "wasted":

1. **You vote for someone who loses.** That vote did not affect the outcome.
2. **You vote for someone who wins by a huge margin.** Your candidate was already winning comfortably. The extra votes beyond what was needed to win did nothing.

A riding map can be designed — intentionally or not — so that one party's supporters end up either concentrated in a few ridings they win by enormous margins (lots of wasted surplus votes) or spread across ridings where they keep losing narrowly (lots of wasted losing votes). Either way, that party needs more total votes to win the same number of seats. Their votes are less efficient.

Here is how the two maps compare, across 896,644 total votes cast for the two main parties:

- Under the **majority map**: NDP voters wasted roughly **881 more votes** than UCP voters. That is about 0.1% of all ballots — nearly equal.
- Under the **minority map**: NDP voters wasted roughly **36,000 more votes** than UCP voters. That is about 4% of all ballots.

The minority map's gap is about **41 times larger** than the majority map's. Both gaps favour the UCP.

**What this means in plain terms:** Under the minority map, NDP voters as a group have to cast significantly more votes to win the same number of seats. The map tilts the playing field.

---

## Finding 4: Two specific choices — Airdrie and northwest Calgary

**Airdrie** is a city of about 85,000 people — larger than Red Deer. It has one city hall, one tax bill, one school board. The majority map puts Airdrie in two ridings. The minority map splits it into four, attaching each piece to a different rural area. With four different MLAs, no single politician is primarily accountable to Airdrie. A citywide problem — a road, a school, a hospital — has to be taken to four different offices, each of which mostly represents somewhere else.

**Northwest Calgary** is a predominantly UCP-supporting area. The minority map packs 11.5% more people than average into northwest Calgary ridings. That means large numbers of UCP votes pile up in ridings the UCP already wins easily — votes that go past the winning threshold and do nothing. Meanwhile, voters in other parts of Calgary have more say per person. The majority map has northwest Calgary only 2.8% above average.

## What this audit does NOT say

**It does not say either map is illegal or a "gerrymander."** A "gerrymander" is an American legal term for a specific kind of unconstitutional riding map. Canada does not have that law. In Canada, the legal question is whether voters have "effective representation" under the *Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms*, and whether the commission followed the *Electoral Boundaries Commission Act*. Whether these maps cross that line is a question for courts and legislators — not this audit.

**It does not say anyone drew the minority map to cheat.** Statistics can show what a map does. They cannot show what anyone intended. The same outcome could come from deliberate choices, from geography, from habit, or from coincidence. This audit measures the effect, not the motive.

**It already accounts for the fact that NDP voters are concentrated in cities.** City voters tend to live close together, which means their votes naturally get bundled into a smaller number of ridings. That gives them a slight built-in disadvantage on any neutral map. The 1 million random maps in the simulation were all drawn using real Alberta geography, so this is factored in. The minority map is still unusual compared to those neutral draws.

## How the analysis was done

All results use the official Elections Alberta riding maps, received May 6, 2026. The 2023 election results were matched to each riding by finding which riding each polling station fell inside.

The 1 million random maps were generated by a standard redistricting research tool (GerryChain). Each random map follows the same population rules the commission had to follow.

All tests were written down and publicly filed **before** the results were examined. This is called "pre-registration." It prevents a researcher from running many tests and only reporting the ones that look good — a common way that statistics can mislead. The registration records are publicly filed at OSF:6pt83, AsPredicted:#289,469, and AsPredicted:#289,451.
