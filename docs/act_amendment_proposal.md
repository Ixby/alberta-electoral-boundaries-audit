# A proposal to amend §12 of the Alberta Electoral Boundaries Commission Act

**Scope.** The *Electoral Boundaries Commission Act*, RSA 2000 c E-3 §12
governs what population data Alberta's electoral boundary commissions
must use. The current §12(3) requires the most recent decennial census;
§12(5) permits supplementation with more recent information "in
conjunction with" the census. The 2025–26 commission's own methodology
cannot be reconciled with a strict reading of these subsections. This
document proposes legislative language that resolves the ambiguity,
either at minimum (Option A) or by acknowledging what the commission
actually does in practice (Option B).

**The audit's stance.** This audit's reformer role is to propose better
law, not to declare existing law broken. §12 is not unworkable — it is
ambiguous. Two generations of Alberta commissions have read the
ambiguity in broadly similar ways, and no court has ruled on it. What
the 2026 cycle shows is that the ambiguity is widening as Alberta's
post-census growth rate rises. A legislature that addresses the
ambiguity now will save future commissions, courts, and petitioners the
cost of resolving it under litigation pressure.

## Phase 1 — Historical rationale: why the decennial census?

### 1.1 Origin of the census-based rule in Canadian electoral law

Before Confederation, the handful of colonial redistributions that
preceded the Province of Canada (1841) and then Confederation (1867)
used the best population counts available — usually a provincial or
colonial enumeration, rarely more than a rough estimate. The British
North America Act, 1867 (later renamed the Constitution Act, 1867)
introduced the census-based rule federally. Section 51 required that
the distribution of seats in the House of Commons be readjusted "on
the completion of the census," which in Canadian practice meant the
decennial census first authorized in 1871.

For the first century after Confederation, federal electoral
redistributions were performed by Parliament itself. Following each
decennial census, Parliament introduced a redistribution Bill, usually
drafted by a select committee of the governing party. This structure
was persistently criticized as a vehicle for gerrymandering: the
elected representatives whose seats were being redrawn had a direct
interest in the boundaries. Wilfrid Laurier's 1903 reform assigned the
drawing task to a special multi-party House of Commons committee,
which reduced but did not eliminate partisan influence.

The structural shift came in 1964, when Parliament passed the
*Electoral Boundaries Readjustment Act*, RSC 1985 c E-3 (enacted as SC
1964-65 c 31). The Act transferred the drawing task from Parliament to
ten independent provincial commissions, one per province, each chaired
by a judge. The Act's data provision tied these commissions directly
to the census: under §13, the Chief Statistician of Canada prepares a
certified return of the population of Canada and of each province "as
ascertained by the census" and transmits it to the Chief Electoral
Officer, who then transmits the province-level breakdown to each
commission. The Act's §15(1)(a) defines the "electoral quota for the
province" as the population of the province "as ascertained by the
census" divided by the number of seats allotted to the province.

The census is built into the federal Act not as one option among
several but as the single authoritative source. A commission does not
choose the data; it receives it.

Provincial adoption followed the federal model at varying speeds.
Alberta's *Electoral Boundaries Commission Act* was enacted in 1990
(SA 1990 c E-4.01, later RSA 2000 c E-3) and used the federal template
as its starting point, including the census-primary structure now
found in §12. British Columbia, Ontario, Quebec, and Saskatchewan
enacted analogous statutes through the 1980s and 1990s.

### 1.2 The 1991 Saskatchewan Reference

The definitive Canadian constitutional case on electoral boundaries is
*Reference re Provincial Electoral Boundaries (Saskatchewan)*, [1991]
2 SCR 158, often cited as "the Carter case" or "the Saskatchewan
Reference." The Saskatchewan Court of Appeal had ruled that the
province's proposed electoral boundaries — with variances up to
fifty percent from the provincial quota — infringed s. 3 of the
Charter. The Supreme Court of Canada, in a 6–3 majority written by
McLachlin J (as she then was), reversed.

The holding is that s. 3 of the Charter protects "effective
representation," not absolute voter parity. In McLachlin J's words:

> The purpose of the right to vote enshrined in s. 3 of the Charter
> is not equality of voting power per se, but the right to "effective
> representation." ... Notwithstanding the fact that the value of a
> citizen's vote should not be unduly diluted, it is a practical fact
> that effective representation often cannot be achieved without
> taking into account countervailing factors.

The Saskatchewan Reference thus operates at a higher level of
abstraction than the data-source question. McLachlin J does not
address which population data is "legitimate." The case concerns
**permissible variance**, not data currency. A commission is free,
under the Reference, to use any reasonable population source; what
the Charter reviews is the resulting dispersion of voter weight. A
commission that draws boundaries with variances inside ±25% of any
reasonable provincial quota is presumptively compliant with s. 3;
variances beyond that threshold require justification in the language
of effective representation (geography, community of interest,
minority representation, sparsity, rate of growth).

For the purposes of this proposal, two things follow. First, the
census is not constitutionally required. Nothing in the Saskatchewan
Reference or its progeny (*Friends of Democracy v Canada*, 2003
ABQB; *Raîche v Canada (Attorney General)*, 2004 FC 679; *Re
Electoral Boundaries Commission Act*, 1991 ABCA 317) compels use of
a specific data source. The constitutional constraint is on the
output (district-population dispersion), not the input. Second, the
legislature is free to choose. Alberta's census-primary rule in
§12(3) is a legislative choice, not a constitutional minimum.

### 1.3 Courtney's treatment of the independent-commission model

John C. Courtney's *Commissioned Ridings: Designing Canada's
Electoral Districts* (McGill-Queen's, 2001) is the authoritative
scholarly treatment of the Canadian independent-commission model. The
book tracks the post-1964 federal transition and the subsequent
provincial adoptions. Courtney's principal concerns are the
composition and independence of the commissions themselves, the
statutory criteria they apply (population equality, community of
interest, geographic factors), and the tension between the
commissions' autonomy and the final parliamentary approval.

Courtney does not treat data currency as a distinct problem. His
analysis assumes the decennial census as the data source, in part
because for most of the twentieth century no realistic alternative
existed at the small-area level the commissions required. Courtney
does flag the timing problem — the interval between the census
reference date and the commission's sitting date — but treats it as a
practical inconvenience rather than a structural defect. Writing in
2000, with Canadian provincial population growth rates in the 1–2%
annual range, Courtney had no empirical reason to flag cycle-lag as
a first-order issue.

The closest Courtney comes to the data-currency question is his
treatment of urban-rural imbalance: commissions have historically
resolved mid-cycle growth in urban fringes by redrawing boundaries at
the next full cycle, not by adjusting data sources within a cycle.
That posture is reasonable when annual growth is 1% and cycles are
ten years; it is much harder to defend when annual growth is 4% and
cycles are ten to fourteen years, which is Alberta's 2026 situation.

### 1.4 Why decennial frequency specifically

Three reasons explain the decennial choice.

First, **authoritativeness**. For most of the twentieth century the
full census was the only population count in Canada with sub-CSD
spatial resolution, legal compulsion of response under the
*Statistics Act*, and nation-wide coverage on a single reference
date. Provincial population estimates existed but were themselves
typically produced by extrapolation from the most recent census. Any
alternative to the census was, in effect, a census derivative.

Second, **methodological consistency**. A ten-year interval
standardizes the frequency of redistribution across the eleven
Canadian boundary authorities (federal plus ten provinces), allowing
comparisons and cross-jurisdictional learning. Switching one
jurisdiction to a different frequency or data source breaks that
consistency.

Third, **cost and capacity**. A full enumeration is an expensive
logistical exercise. A decennial frequency balances data freshness
against the fiscal and human resources available.

### 1.5 What changed since 2010

Three developments have altered the 2010–2026 environment against
which the census-primary rule was designed.

**Alberta Treasury Board and Finance quarterly estimates.** TBF's
Office of Statistics and Information produces quarterly provincial
and municipal-level population estimates, updated continuously,
available through the *Alberta Regional Dashboard* and the open.
alberta.ca data portal. The methodology (component-cohort-survival
models calibrated against StatsCan vital statistics and migration
records, with documented low/medium/high scenarios) is publicly
documented. TBF's data is the data the 2025–26 Alberta commission in
fact used — the July 1, 2024 estimate of 4,888,723 is a TBF figure,
not a census figure.

**Alberta Health Care Insurance Plan (AHCIP) administrative
records.** AHCIP data, anchored to near-universal enrolment of
Alberta residents, provides a de facto population register updated
continuously. It has limitations (temporary residents, non-filers,
inter-provincial migrants in transit) but it is a real
administrative data source that did not exist in usable form when
§12 was drafted.

**Statistics Canada's Administrative Data Census pilot and the
Canadian Statistical Demographic Database (CSDD).** StatsCan itself
has, since roughly 2015, actively explored administrative-data-based
population estimation as a potential supplement or eventual
replacement for the quinquennial census. The CSDD links federal and
provincial administrative records to produce a continuous estimate
of the resident population and its usual place of residence. The
United Kingdom and New Zealand are pursuing similar models with the
explicit aim of replacing their traditional censuses. StatsCan has
not committed to that path, but the Canadian census is no longer the
only nationally-authoritative population-count source — and by 2030
may not even be the primary one.

**Summary of Phase 1.** The decennial-census rule was the right
choice in 1964 and a defensible choice through roughly 2010. It
reflected a world in which the census was the only nationally
authoritative population source, growth rates were low enough that
a ten-year cycle produced tolerable staleness, and no administrative
alternative was mature enough to supplement it. Alberta has since
moved outside all three of those conditions. A legislature drafting
§12 from scratch in 2026 would not write the rule the way it is
written now.

## Phase 2 — Comparative scan: six Canadian jurisdictions

Each entry summarizes what the statute says, what the commission
actually used, and any documented dispute on the data source. A
verdict is assigned: **REQUIRES** (statute mandates census),
**PERMITS SUPPLEMENT** (census primary, alternatives allowed),
**EXPLICITLY ALLOWS ALTERNATIVES** (statute contemplates non-census
data as primary), or **SILENT** (no data-source rule).

### 2.1 Federal: Electoral Boundaries Readjustment Act (RSC 1985 c E-3)

**Statute.** §13 vests the Chief Statistician with the duty to
certify the population of Canada and of each province "as ascertained
by the census" and transmit it to the Chief Electoral Officer. §14
directs the Chief Electoral Officer to calculate provincial seat
allocations from that return. §15(1)(a) defines the "electoral quota
for the province" as the census population divided by the seat
allotment. Census-data-primary throughout.

**Most recent redistribution.** 2022. All ten provincial commissions
used the 2021 decennial census. The federal Act does not permit
supplementation of the census with newer estimates at the boundary-
drawing stage — commissions work from the census figures as certified
by the Chief Statistician.

**Documented dispute on data source.** None specific to data
currency. The 2022 redistribution produced substantial litigation
and political controversy over specific provincial allocations and
individual district boundaries (Bill C-14 amended s. 51 of the
Constitution Act to protect Quebec's seat count), but no challenge
questioned the use of 2021 census data itself.

**Verdict:** **REQUIRES.** Federal statute uses census data
exclusively, with no supplementation provision.

### 2.2 British Columbia: Electoral Boundaries Commission Act (RSBC 1996 c 107)

**Statute.** The Act defines "population of British Columbia" and
"electoral quotient" in §9 without specifying a data source. The
commission is governed by effective-representation principles (§9),
with no mandatory census reference. On a plain reading, the Act
leaves the data source to the commission's discretion.

**Most recent redistribution.** 2023. The BC commission used BC
Stats population estimates as its primary basis, anchored to the
2021 Canadian census but updated to reflect post-census growth. The
commission's final report derives the electoral quotient (53,773) and
the ±25% band (40,330 to 67,216) from estimates that post-date the
2021 census. The 2023 redistribution increased BC from 87 to 93
seats.

**Documented dispute on data source.** None. BC's discretionary
approach has operated without significant legal or political
challenge through multiple redistribution cycles.

**Verdict:** **SILENT / EXPLICITLY ALLOWS ALTERNATIVES.** The BC
statute does not specify a data source. In practice, BC uses the
most-recent provincial estimate, including post-census updates.

### 2.3 Ontario: Representation Act (SO 2005 c 35)

**Statute.** The Act provides for the drawing of southern Ontario
ridings (96 as of 2005; now most of Ontario's 124 ridings) to be
coextensive with the federal ridings drawn under the federal EBRA.
In effect, Ontario provincial boundaries in the south *inherit* the
federal commission's data choice. For the 11 northern ridings,
preserved by statutory carve-out, older boundaries remain in force.
Ontario's provincial boundary-drawing thus rides on the federal
census-primary rule by construction.

**Most recent redistribution.** 2018 (following the 2015 federal
redistribution, itself based on the 2011 census). Ontario's southern
ridings therefore use 2011 census data, not 2021 — because the
federal redraw that generates them is upstream. The 2022 federal
redistribution will flow through to Ontario in due course.

**Documented dispute on data source.** None on the data source
itself. The *Far North Electoral Boundaries Commission* (2017)
raised Indigenous-representation concerns distinct from the
data-source question.

**Verdict:** **REQUIRES** (by inheritance from federal EBRA).
Ontario's provincial boundaries in the south are federally drawn
from census data.

### 2.4 Quebec: Loi sur la représentation électorale (RLRQ c R-24.1)

**Statute.** Quebec's Act is distinctive. The *Commission de la
représentation électorale* is an institutional body with continuing
existence (not an ad hoc commission per cycle), and the Act's
§14–§15 allow the Commission to work from "the number of electors"
rather than resident population. The electoral list is the
operative dataset, updated continuously by Élections Québec from
provincial administrative sources (driver licence, health card,
tax records). Census data is used as a background reference but is
not the primary basis for boundary-drawing.

**Most recent redistribution.** 2017. The Commission used the
list of electors at January 31, 2017 as the primary basis. Census
data (2011 at the time) informed the background analysis of
population trends but did not directly drive district size.

**Documented dispute on data source.** None. Quebec's elector-based
model has operated continuously since 1980 and is the longest-
running Canadian deviation from census-primary redistribution.

**Verdict:** **EXPLICITLY ALLOWS ALTERNATIVES.** Quebec uses
administrative electoral lists rather than census data.

### 2.5 Saskatchewan: Constituency Boundaries Act (SS 1993 c C-27.1)

**Statute.** The Act requires that commissions be struck after each
decennial census. §17 defines the "constituency population quotient"
using the census figures received from Statistics Canada. The Act
applies an unusually tight ±5% variance window (rather than the ±25%
used federally and in most provinces), and requires the commission
to work from the census figures delivered at the commission's
constitution.

**Most recent redistribution.** 2022. The commission used 2021
census data delivered at the April 27, 2022 constitution date. The
Act's mandatory ±5% window gave the commission almost no discretion
to supplement the census; any supplementation would have had to fit
within a much tighter variance envelope than Alberta's ±25%.

**Documented dispute on data source.** None. The Saskatchewan
approach has the strongest statutory discipline of any Canadian
jurisdiction and has not been challenged on the data-source
question.

**Verdict:** **REQUIRES.** Saskatchewan uses census data with no
supplementation provision and the tightest variance window in
Canada.

### 2.6 Alberta: Electoral Boundaries Commission Act (RSA 2000 c E-3)

**Statute.** §12(3) requires the most recent decennial census as
primary. §12(5) permits supplementation with more recent
information "in conjunction with" the decennial data. The Act is
permissive about supplementation but textually treats the census as
the anchor.

**Most recent redistribution.** 2025–26 (in progress). Both the
majority and minority commission reports describe their basis as
"the 2021 decennial census updated to a July 1, 2024 estimate" but
in fact derive all per-ED figures and the provincial quota
(54,929) from the July 2024 TBF estimate of 4,888,723 — not from
the 2021 census of 4,262,635. The audit's Track K documents this
in detail.

**Documented dispute on data source.** The Track K finding is the
first systematic audit flagging the gap between stated and actual
methodology. No formal court challenge has been filed as of
2026-04-22. The ABlawg commentary on the minority report (April
2026) discusses related concerns.

**Verdict:** **PERMITS SUPPLEMENT** (formally); **EXPLICITLY
ALLOWS ALTERNATIVES** in practice by the commission's own reading.

### 2.7 Comparative table

| Jurisdiction  | Statute verdict              | Actual practice (most recent) | Dispute |
|---------------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| Federal       | REQUIRES                     | 2021 census, unsupplemented   | None    |
| British Columbia | SILENT                    | BC Stats estimate (post-census) | None  |
| Ontario       | REQUIRES (via federal inherit) | 2011 census (via 2015 fed redraw) | None |
| Quebec        | EXPLICITLY ALLOWS ALTERNATIVES | Elector list (2017)          | None    |
| Saskatchewan  | REQUIRES                     | 2021 census, unsupplemented   | None    |
| Alberta       | PERMITS SUPPLEMENT           | 2024 TBF estimate (unsupplemented census present) | Track K of this audit |

**Key observations.** Three jurisdictions (federal, Saskatchewan,
Ontario-by-inheritance) require census data and use it unsupplemented.
Two (British Columbia, Quebec) allow and use alternatives openly.
Alberta is unique in two respects: (i) its statute contemplates
supplementation but textually anchors on the census, and (ii) its
commission in practice uses the supplement as the primary basis while
describing the methodology as census-anchored. The audit's Track K
finding is therefore a symptom of Alberta's position between the
two clusters rather than an Alberta-specific deviance.

## Phase 3 — The empirical anchor: §12(3) is already being overridden

The Track K analysis documents the following facts. They are
summarized here to motivate the reform proposal; the full
documentation is in `analysis/reports/plan_b_cross_check.md`.

1. **Stated versus actual basis.** The 2025–26 commission's majority
   report (p. 29) and minority report (p. 297) both describe their
   basis as "the 2021 decennial census updated to a July 1, 2024
   estimate by the Alberta Treasury Board's Office of Statistics and
   Information." The per-ED population figures in both reports, and
   the Alberta-wide total of 4,888,723 from which the provincial
   quota of 54,929 is derived, are the TBF July 2024 estimate. The
   2021 census figure of 4,262,635 appears only as historical
   context in the narrative; it does not enter the variance
   calculations.

2. **Derivation of the ±25% window.** The floor (41,197) and
   ceiling (68,661) of the permitted variance band are derived from
   the 2024 estimate, not from the 2021 census. A band derived from
   the census would be 31,969 to 53,283 per ED — materially
   different.

3. **Every district's stated deviation is computed against a
   non-census mean.** Each of the 89 per-district deviation
   percentages in both Appendix A (majority) and Appendix E
   (minority) is computed relative to the 2024-based mean of 54,929.
   The commission's internal variance reporting is therefore built
   entirely on the 2024 supplement.

4. **Reading §12(5) narrowly versus broadly.**
   - **Narrow reading** ("in conjunction with" means *as a supplement
     to*, not *instead of*): the commission has not in fact produced
     a census-anchored base with a 2024 supplement applied to it.
     The variance calculations would not clearly fit within a
     narrow reading of §12(3) and §12(5).
   - **Broad reading** ("in conjunction with" means *with the
     census somewhere in the background analysis*): the commission
     is within §12(5). On this reading, however, §12(3)'s mandatory
     language has no operative force — any plausible commission
     could treat the census as background and the estimate as
     primary.

The audit does not take a position on which reading is correct.
That is a court's determination. What the audit can say as a matter
of fact is that the commission's §III text describes a methodology
that the commission's tables do not implement, and that a narrow
reading of §12(3)–(5) would not clearly accommodate what the
tables in fact do.

**Why this motivates reform.** Regardless of whether the commission
is within §12(5) on a generous reading, the 2026 cycle has surfaced
a statutory tension that will recur and intensify. Alberta's
2021–mid-2025 growth rate of 17.8% (Track L, `analysis/v0_1_cycle_
lag_analysis.md`) makes the gap between census and estimate
structurally larger than it was in the 2010 or 2017 cycles. By
2030 or 2035, with peak cycle lag approaching 14 years, the gap
will be larger still. A statute that requires the commission to
cite the census while permitting it to use the estimate places
every future commission in the same bind: describe one method,
implement another, hope no one notices. Reform fixes the bind at
the drafting level, before the next cycle.

## Phase 4 — Caveats and objections reformers must answer

Five arguments against amending §12. For each, the proposal assigns
one of three verdicts: **(a) decisive** (the objection defeats the
reform), **(b) addressable through drafting** (the objection can be
accommodated in amendment language), or **(c) acceptable cost for
the reform benefit**.

### 4.1 The census is constitutionally anchored through the Statistics Act

**Objection.** The decennial census is a federal statutory
institution under the *Statistics Act*, RSC 1985 c S-19. It carries
legal compulsion of response, prescribed methodology, and long
historical legitimacy. Provincial estimates and administrative data
sources, however well-designed, are not constitutionally or federally
anchored in the same way.

**Response.** True, but not decisive. The Saskatchewan Reference
does not require a specific data source; it requires effective
representation, which is an output constraint. Nothing in Canadian
constitutional law prevents a province from using data sources other
than the census. Quebec has done so continuously since 1980
without constitutional challenge. BC has done so since the 1990s.
Alberta would not be breaking new constitutional ground by using
non-census data — only by being explicit about it.

**Verdict:** **(c) acceptable cost for the reform benefit.** Moving
to a non-census primary basis requires forfeiting one layer of
federal anchoring. The tradeoff is worth the gain in data currency.

### 4.2 Provincial estimates are politically vulnerable

**Objection.** TBF is currently a well-resourced agency with a
documented methodology and no record of political interference.
But a future government could influence its own population
estimates in ways the federal Chief Statistician cannot be
influenced. A statute that pins boundary-drawing to provincial
estimates is robust only against current conditions, not future
bad-faith actors.

**Response.** This is the strongest objection. The argument is not
that TBF is currently untrustworthy — it is that statutory rules
must survive adversarial interpretation by less-scrupulous
governments, and the rule "whatever the provincial agency says"
does not.

**Addressable through drafting.** The reform can (and under
Option B does) require (i) the commission to use TBF estimates
*in conjunction with* StatsCan quarterly provincial estimates
(Table 17-10-0009); (ii) a binding tie-breaker where the two
diverge beyond ±2%, typically defaulting to the more conservative
figure; and (iii) public disclosure of any adjustment or
methodology change made by TBF during the cycle.

**Verdict:** **(b) addressable through drafting.** The
reform must incorporate a non-provincial check on provincial data.

### 4.3 Federal-provincial consistency

**Objection.** Federal redistribution uses the census. A province
that moves to non-census data breaks cross-jurisdictional
comparability: Alberta's ±25% window, Saskatchewan's ±5% window,
and the federal commission's calculations will no longer use a
common population base.

**Response.** Federal-provincial data alignment is already uneven.
Quebec has used the electoral list rather than the census for
decades. British Columbia uses BC Stats estimates. The "common
base" is already a convenient fiction. Moreover, provincial and
federal commissions draw different maps for different purposes;
they were never constrained to use the same inputs.

**Verdict:** **(c) acceptable cost.** Alberta's comparability with
federal redistribution is weakened. Comparability with BC and
Quebec improves. Comparability with Saskatchewan and the federal
commission is unchanged in the direction that matters (both remain
census-based; Alberta moves away).

### 4.4 Stakeholder reliance on the interpretive tradition

**Objection.** A body of interpretation — cases, commentaries,
Elections Alberta guidance — has developed around the census-primary
rule. Amending §12 changes the interpretive base and unsettles
expectations for lawyers, courts, and petitioners.

**Response.** Very little Alberta-specific interpretation actually
depends on §12's data-source rule. The Saskatchewan Reference,
*Friends of Democracy v Canada*, and *Raîche v Canada (Attorney
General)* all address effective representation rather than data
sources. The precedent that matters for §12 is effectively
empty — there is no case law interpreting §12(5)'s "in conjunction
with" clause.

**Verdict:** **(c) acceptable cost.** The interpretive tradition
being unsettled is thin.

### 4.5 Inter-jurisdictional comparability

**Objection.** A census-based rule lets Alberta's redistribution
be evaluated on the same dataset as other Canadian commissions.
Changing the rule changes that cross-check.

**Response.** Same as 4.3. Comparability across Canadian
commissions is already weak. The Saskatchewan Reference analysis
compares redistributions on *variance*, not on *population data
source*. A 2024-based Alberta map and a 2021-based federal
Alberta map can be compared on their deviation distributions
without using the same data input.

**Verdict:** **(c) acceptable cost.**

### 4.6 Summary of Phase 4

No objection is decisive. One objection (4.2 — political
vulnerability of provincial data) is substantive and must be
answered in the drafting. Three (4.1, 4.3, 4.5) are acceptable
costs. One (4.4) is thin. The reform is therefore defensible on the
merits provided the drafting includes the non-provincial-agency
check required by 4.2.

## Phase 5 — Proposed amendment text

Two options are drafted. Option A is the minimum viable reform
that clarifies the current ambiguity without changing the
substance of §12(3). Option B is the structural reform that
acknowledges what commissions actually do in practice and builds
the discipline around that.

### 5.1 Option A — minor reform (clarify §12(5))

**Proposed statutory text.** Amend §12 by replacing subsection (5)
with the following and adding subsections (6) and (7):

> **12 (5)** The Commission may, where it considers that more
> recent information will better serve the principles of effective
> representation and population equality, use more recent
> information respecting the population of all or any part of
> Alberta in conjunction with the information referred to in
> subsection (3) or (4), provided that
>
> (a) the Commission computes the provincial quota, the variance
> band under section 14, and the eligibility thresholds under
> section 15(2) using the information referred to in subsection (3)
> or (4) as the primary basis; and
>
> (b) where the Commission also reports per-electoral-division
> figures derived from more recent information, it reports the
> corresponding figures derived from the information referred to
> in subsection (3) or (4) in the same table, clearly labelled,
> and discloses the sources, reference dates, and methodology of
> both.
>
> **12 (6)** Where the Commission exercises the discretion under
> subsection (5), it shall include in its final report a statement
> identifying
>
> (a) each subsection-(5) information source used;
>
> (b) the reference date and publisher of each source;
>
> (c) the reason the Commission considers that use of that source
> better serves the principles of effective representation and
> population equality; and
>
> (d) for each proposed electoral division where the legal-window
> status under section 15 would differ between the information
> referred to in subsection (3) or (4) and the more recent
> information, the Commission's reasoning for relying on the
> source on which it has in fact relied.
>
> **12 (7)** For greater certainty, the variance band in section
> 14 and the eligibility thresholds in section 15(2) are not
> varied by the use of more recent information under subsection
> (5).

**Consequential amendments.**

- **§14 (population-equality window).** Add "computed in accordance
  with section 12(5)(a)" after "average population of all the
  proposed electoral divisions" in the 25% deviation rule.
- **§15(2) (exceptions).** Add "computed in accordance with
  section 12(5)(a)" to the eligibility threshold language.
- **§13 (Commission's final report).** Add a requirement that the
  report include the disclosure statement under §12(6).

**Fit with 2026 practice.** Under Option A, the 2025–26
commission's present methodology would need to be revised. The
provincial quota and variance band would have to be recomputed
from the 2021 census figure (4,262,635 / 89 = 47,894 quota; floor
35,921; ceiling 59,868). The 2024 TBF-derived figures would appear
alongside but would not be the basis of the legal-window
determination. Several districts that currently sit inside ±25%
on the 2024 base would fall outside ±25% on the 2021 census base —
or vice versa — and the commission would have to adjudicate each.
This is the clarifying function of Option A: it forces the
commission to be explicit about which base governs legal-window
status, which under Option A is the census.

### 5.2 Option B — structural reform (replace §12(3))

**Proposed statutory text.** Amend §12 by replacing subsections
(1) through (5) with the following:

> **12 (1)** For the purposes of this Part, the population of
> Alberta and of each proposed electoral division is to be
> determined by the Commission in accordance with this section.
>
> **(2)** In this section,
>
> (a) "decennial census" means the most recent decennial census
> of population referred to in section 19(3) of the *Statistics
> Act* (Canada) from which the population of all proposed
> electoral divisions is available;
>
> (b) "federal quarterly estimate" means the most recent
> quarterly estimate of provincial population published by
> Statistics Canada in Table 17-10-0009 or its successor;
>
> (c) "provincial quarterly estimate" means the most recent
> quarterly estimate of provincial and sub-provincial population
> published by the Alberta Treasury Board and Finance Office of
> Statistics and Information;
>
> (d) "composite basis" means a population figure for Alberta or
> for any proposed electoral division derived from the provincial
> quarterly estimate, calibrated to the federal quarterly
> estimate in accordance with subsection (5).
>
> **(3)** The Commission is to use the composite basis as the
> primary source for
>
> (a) the total population of Alberta;
>
> (b) the provincial quota under section 14;
>
> (c) the variance band under section 14; and
>
> (d) the per-electoral-division populations used to assess each
> proposed electoral division against the variance band and the
> section 15(2) eligibility thresholds.
>
> **(4)** Where the population of one or more proposed electoral
> divisions cannot be reliably derived on the composite basis
> because of the geographic granularity of available data, the
> Commission may use the decennial census as the sole basis for
> those electoral divisions, identifying each such division in
> its final report.
>
> **(5)** The composite basis is derived by the Commission as
> follows:
>
> (a) where the provincial quarterly estimate of Alberta's total
> population differs from the federal quarterly estimate of
> Alberta's total population by no more than 2 percent, the
> Commission uses the provincial quarterly estimate;
>
> (b) where the two differ by more than 2 percent, the
> Commission uses the arithmetic mean of the two, and shall state
> the discrepancy and its methodology in its final report;
>
> (c) the Commission shall publish, as part of its final report
> and in a machine-readable form on the Commission's public
> website, the population figure used for every proposed
> electoral division, together with the underlying source data
> and the method of aggregation, in sufficient detail that any
> member of the public can reproduce the figure.
>
> **(6)** For greater certainty, the Commission shall also
> publish the population of Alberta and of each proposed
> electoral division as derived from the decennial census, in
> the same table and with the same presentation standards as the
> composite basis, so that a reader can compare the two.
>
> **(7)** Where the legal-window status of a proposed electoral
> division under section 14 or section 15(2) would differ on the
> composite basis from the decennial census, the Commission's
> final report shall identify the division and explain its choice
> of basis.

**Consequential amendments.**

- **§13 (Commission's final report).** Expand to require
  publication of the composite-basis data, the census data, and
  the reconciliation statement under §12(5) and §12(7).
- **§14 (population-equality window).** Clarify that the ±25%
  window is applied to the composite basis.
- **§15(2) (exceptions).** Clarify that the eligibility
  thresholds are applied to the composite basis.
- **§17 (publication).** Add a requirement that the commission's
  population dataset be published in a machine-readable format on
  the commission's website, reproducible from named open sources.
- **New §12.1 (public right of reproduction).** "Any member of
  the public is entitled, on request, to receive the population
  dataset used by the Commission in a form that permits the
  dataset to be independently reproduced from published sources."

**Fit with 2026 practice.** Option B largely ratifies what the
2025–26 commission has in fact done — use TBF's 2024 estimate — but
tightens it with a StatsCan cross-check, a mandatory reproducibility
publication, and a paired census-based table for comparison. Under
Option B, the commission would not need to revise its §III
narrative; it would only need to add the paired census-based
calculation (already computable from the published StatsCan data)
and the reproducibility dataset. The existing variance band of
41,197 to 68,661 would stand, provided the 2024 TBF and StatsCan
Q2-2024 estimates for Alberta differ by less than 2%. (At the time
of writing, they did, so Option B formally ratifies the
commission's present basis.)

|--------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------|

## Phase 6 — Implementation notes: what the 2026 committee can do now

The MLA Special Select Committee chaired by Brandon Lunty (due
November 2, 2026) cannot amend the Act within its own report. It
can, however, adopt the *discipline* of either option without
waiting for the legislature. Two specific steps are recommended.

### 6.1 Publish paired population tables

For every ED in the committee's proposed 91-seat map, publish
both a decennial-census-derived population figure and a composite-
basis-derived population figure. Show both in every variance
table. Flag each ED where the two figures place the district on
opposite sides of the ±25% window, or on opposite sides of the
s.15(2) eligibility threshold. The flagged-ED list is the
committee's audit trail: any reader can see where the choice of
basis matters and why the committee chose what it chose.

### 6.2 State the choice of primary basis explicitly

If the committee's final report derives its ±25% window from one
basis (say, the 2024 TBF estimate), say so in so many words on
the first page of the population-equality chapter:

> "The Committee derives the provincial quota of X, the variance
> band of [floor] to [ceiling], and each district's deviation
> percentage from [2024 TBF estimate / 2021 decennial census /
> composite basis]. Paired figures from the other basis are
> reported in Appendix Z for comparison. Where the choice of
> basis changes a district's legal-window status, the Committee
> has relied on [primary basis] for reasons given in §[X.X]."

This gives readers the tools to audit the committee either way —
which is what this audit has in fact done. The committee's
transparency obligation is discharged by making the choice
explicit, not by hiding the choice behind narrative language.

### 6.3 Cost of adopting this posture

Low. The committee already receives TBF data in the normal course
of its work. The census data is a free public dataset from
StatsCan. The paired table is a straightforward spreadsheet
operation — the audit's own `analysis/reports/plan_b_cross_check.md`
demonstrates the method at the contested-configuration level;
extending it to all 91 EDs is a two-hour exercise.

### 6.4 Political posture

Adopting this discipline does not commit the committee to either
amendment option. It commits the committee only to transparency
about the choice being made. A committee that publishes both
bases gives its successors (and any subsequent legislative
reform) the information needed to decide which basis should be
statutorily mandated. A committee that does not publish both
bases is repeating the 2025–26 commission's characterization
issue: describing one methodology, implementing another.

## Author-level recommendation

The reform proposal treats Options A and B as alternatives, but
the audit's recommendation is Option B. Three reasons.

First, **Option B accepts reality.** The 2025–26 commission has
already operated on what is, in effect, a composite basis. Option
B formalizes the practice and disciplines it. Option A rejects
the practice and forces future commissions back to a census-
primary rule whose timing disadvantage will only worsen over the
next two cycles.

Second, **Option B addresses the political-vulnerability
objection (4.2) better than Option A**. The ±2% StatsCan tie-
breaker built into Option B §12(5)(a)–(b) is a real constraint
on provincial-agency manipulation. Option A has no comparable
safeguard because it leaves the primary basis as the
federally-controlled census.

Third, **Option B's public-reproduction requirement (§12.1) is
worth the drafting cost.** Any statutory reform of §12 is an
opportunity to bake in reproducibility discipline that does not
currently exist anywhere in the Canadian electoral-boundary
framework. Option A does not create that opportunity. Option B
does.

The case for Option A is honest about the one advantage it has:
it is a smaller change. A legislature uncomfortable with
structural reform could adopt Option A as an interim measure,
with a sunset clause triggering review at the 2035 cycle. On
balance, however, Option B is the more durable choice. The
audit recommends Option B.

## Addendum (Session 9, Track N + Track O) — concrete Option B specification and disclosure-requirement clauses

Phase 5's Option B drafted text specifies the statute's structure but leaves the composite-basis component sources implicit. Two parallel sub-tracks in session 9 — Track N (Alberta government database survey) and Track O (commission source provenance audit) — produced concrete recommendations that tighten Option B's §12(5) and add a new disclosure-requirement section §12(6)–(7). Neither addendum changes Phase 5's drafted text; both provide the specificity a legislative drafter would need to implement Option B.

### A. Composite-basis source specification (Track N)

**Context.** Track N's survey of Alberta government population-data sources (`analysis/methodology/alberta_government_databases_survey.md`) tested eleven candidate systems for reliability, independence, completeness, and constitutional suitability. A critical finding: **census-based redistribution is not constitutionally required for provincial elections.** Constitution Act 1867 §51 governs federal redistribution only. Provincial boundary jurisdiction sits under §92(1) / §45 of the Constitution Act 1982. Charter §3 (via *Reference re Saskatchewan*) operates on output (effective representation) not input (data source). The data-source choice is a policy question.

**Option B §12(5) concrete specification.** Track N ranks three sources for inclusion:

1. **Primary — Alberta Treasury Board and Finance Quarterly Population Estimate (Office of Statistics and Information).** Best combination of CSD-level granularity, quarterly frequency, documented methodology (component-cohort-survival modelling calibrated to vital statistics and administrative records), and 100% population coverage by construction. The commission's 2025–26 practice already relied on this source for per-ED figures (verified by Track K and Track O). Statutory naming aligns the statute with existing commission practice rather than requiring a new data pipeline. §12(2)(c) in the drafted Option B should name TBF's Quarterly Population Estimate publication specifically.

2. **Tie-breaker — Statistics Canada Table 17-10-0009 Quarterly Provincial Population Estimates.** ±2% divergence threshold. If TBF and StatsCan provincial totals differ by more than 2% for Alberta, the StatsCan estimate governs; divergence below 2% defaults to TBF. §12(2)(b) should name this table specifically. (Note: in the current cycle TBF and StatsCan agree by construction because OSI nests its provincial control in the StatsCan total — §12(2)(b) is therefore a forward-looking safeguard against future provincial-methodology drift.)

3. **Mandatory cross-check — federal and independent administrative data.** AHCIP registrations (Alberta Health Care Insurance Plan, ~99% coverage at LGA sub-zone geography) and CRA T1 Family File taxfiler data (via StatsCan Table 11-10-0109, federally administered, FSA/CSD granularity). Material divergences (defined as per-ED variance exceeding 10% against the primary basis) must be publicly disclosed in the commission's final report with explanation.

**Certifying authority.** The Chief Electoral Officer of Alberta is the recommended §12(8) certifier. The CEO is a statutorily-independent Officer of the Legislature under the *Election Act* with existing authority to retain external demographic expertise. Recommended over the Auditor General (outside the AG's core competency) and over a new Independent Statistics Officer (higher legislative cost; viable as a follow-up reform). §12(8) in Option B should specify the CEO as the certifier and grant explicit authority to retain independent statistical consultants.

### B. Disclosure-requirement clauses (Track O)

**Context.** Track O's provenance audit (`analysis/methodology/commission_source_provenance.md`) extracted the commission's verbatim data-source disclosures and cross-checked them against the actual arithmetic in the commission's per-ED tables. The audit identified zero major inconsistencies but four material and three minor ones. The material inconsistencies include (i) the "2021 decennial census updated to July 2024" characterization when tables use the July 2024 OSI estimate directly with no visible decennial-census arithmetic, (ii) the absence of a published DA-level dataset and DA-to-ED crosswalk required for reproducibility, (iii) the absence of a visibly-disclosed Indian reserve population supplement required by §12(3)(b) and §12(4)(d), and (iv) a 50-person gap between the minority's stated provincial total and the sum of its per-ED table (4,888,723 vs 4,888,773; 0.001%, immaterial in effect but indicative of disclosure discipline).

**Proposed §12(6) — Primary disclosure requirements.** A commission must publish, as part of its final report:

- (a) **Source publication, date, catalogue number, and URL** for every dataset used in population calculation. For StatsCan: table number, DGUID level, release date. For TBF: quarterly publication, reference date, file name. For on-reserve supplement: CIRNAC file, reference date.
- (b) **Separate disclosure of each source when a figure is a combination.** If the provincial total is a composite (e.g., "StatsCan provincial + CIRNAC reserve supplement"), the components must be disclosed separately with arithmetic.
- (c) **Dissemination area to electoral division crosswalk.** The commission must publish the CSD-level and DA-level population counts it aggregated and the crosswalk used to assign them to EDs. The crosswalk must permit third-party reproduction of per-ED totals.
- (d) **Versioned DA-level dataset release** in machine-readable format (CSV, with UTF-8 encoding and a schema document).
- (e) **Per-First Nation reserve supplement breakdown** as required by the Act's §12(3)(b) / §12(4)(d) reference to populations on First Nations lands, with the CIRNAC file date and community-by-community counts.
- (f) **Per-ED figures must sum exactly to the stated provincial total** (to the person); any rounding convention must be disclosed.
- (g) **Basis characterization must match table arithmetic.** If tables use a postcensal estimate as the primary basis, the prose cannot describe the basis as "decennial census updated to" without showing the arithmetic of that update.

**Proposed §12(7) — Reproducibility requirements.** A third party with only the published documents must be able to:

- (a) Recover the provincial total from named external sources.
- (b) Reproduce any per-ED figure from the published CSD/DA aggregation.
- (c) Verify s.15(2) eligibility determinations with the published methodology.

**Proposed §12(8) — Certification.** The Chief Electoral Officer of Alberta certifies compliance with §12(2), §12(6), and §12(7) before the commission's final report is tabled. Non-compliance triggers a 30-day remediation period; uncorrected non-compliance requires the commission to publish a supplement addressing the deficiencies.

### C. Integration with Phase 5 Option B

These addendum clauses attach to Option B's drafted structure as follows:

- §12(2): add named-source specifications per (A) above.
- §12(5): add the ±2% StatsCan tie-breaker and the mandatory cross-check clause per (A) above.
- §12(6)–(7): add as new subsections per (B) above.
- §12(8): add as new subsection per (A) above's certifying-authority recommendation.

The Phase 5 drafted Option B text remains authoritative; this addendum specifies the statutory content that drafter annotations left abstract. Option A ("minor reform") is unaffected by this addendum. A drafter implementing Option B should treat Phase 5 as the structural template and this addendum as the content specification.
