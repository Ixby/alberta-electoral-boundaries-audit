What this document is, and is not

This is: a reproducible comparison of two proposed maps for Alberta's electoral districts, using public data. It applies the same tests to both maps. It tries to find gaps in its own reasoning before publishing. It explains, in plain language, what the numbers say and what they don't.

This is not a proof that anyone acted in bad faith, a prediction of the 2027 election, a legal opinion, or a final document. Several pieces of data have not yet been released by Elections Alberta; the analysis will be updated when they are.

If you are looking for proof of a scandal, this document won't give you one. If you are looking for proof that nothing unusual is happening, this document won't give you that either. What it gives you is the measurable evidence, with the uncertainty spelled out honestly.

What you can and cannot reliably take from this

What holds up under every check

  • The minority map has wider population swings between districts than the majority map.
  • The minority splits Airdrie across four districts; the majority splits it across two.
  • Three minority districts (Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane, Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park, Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury) have shapes the commission chair himself flagged as engineered. Direct inspection confirms his description.
  • On April 16, 2026, the government rejected the majority map and set up a UCP-majority MLA committee to draft a replacement.
  • The chair's claim that the minority had "no public support" for its five disputed configurations is partly wrong. A search of the 1,340+ public submissions found clear public support for three of five (Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park, Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury, Red Deer hybrids). For two (Airdrie 4-way, Nolan Hill-Cochrane) the chair's claim holds.

What you cannot reliably take

  • You cannot claim the minority map would give the UCP a specific number of extra seats in 2027. The range is 1 to 3 seats in a tied election using 2023 vote patterns; the direction flips under 2019 patterns.
  • You cannot claim the minority is an intentional gerrymander. The audit measures effects, not intent.
  • You cannot claim the majority is "neutral" in an absolute sense. It preserves the 2019 baseline, which has a mild UCP tilt from natural geography.
  • You cannot claim the government's April 16 action is without precedent. It is more interventionist than the three most-cited Canadian comparators but not necessarily unprecedented overall.

Claims in public debate, and what the data supports

Demonstrable (supported by data): minority has wider population swings; minority splits Airdrie more; three minority districts have unusual shapes; April 16 is government-controlled drafting; chair's "no public support" claim was overbroad.

Probable (directionally supported, with uncertainty): minority is modestly more UCP-favourable than majority under 2023 electorate; 1–3 seat difference in a tied election; Alberta's 2019 map has a mild UCP tilt largely from geography.

Unlikely (not supported, sometimes contradicted): "extreme gerrymander" (below US threshold); "no meaningful difference between maps" (six independent tests show differences); "majority is partisan for NDP" (evidence contradicts); "no public support for any minority configuration" (contradicted for three of five); "definite constitutional violation" (not established by this audit).

If the 2027 election is close

The audit's 1 to 3 seat map effect is abstract. Here is what it means in a close-election scenario, which recent polling and political analysts suggest is plausible for 2027.

  • In 2023, 14 ridings were decided by less than 3 percentage points of two-party margin. That is roughly double 2019 (7) or 2015 (8). Most of the 14 are in Calgary.
  • A 1.5 pp swing (the midpoint of the audit's map-effect estimate) applied to 2023 flips 6 seats UCP (Calgary-Acadia, Glenmore, Foothills, Edgemont, Beddington, Banff-Kananaskis) or 4 NDP (Calgary-North West, North, Bow, Lethbridge-East).
  • If 2027 is within 5 seats of a tie, the 1–3 seat map effect is exactly the size that decides whether a party governs alone, governs in a minority, or sits in opposition.

The audit cannot predict the 2027 election. The map effect is small, consistent, and directionally UCP-favourable under 2023 voter conditions. Whether it matters for government formation depends entirely on how close the actual vote is.

What is missing from the dataset

The 2026 boundary shapefiles have not been released. About 88 of 1,340 public submissions are scans that the text search could not read. The audit does not have imagery for roughly three-quarters of the majority map outside Calgary. The partisan-bias numbers depend on a 70/30 urban/rural modelling assumption; testing across 0.60 / 0.70 / 0.80 shows direction stable but magnitude variable. The 2015 election used different district boundaries than 2019, so 2015 vote data could not plug directly into cross-election checks.

Scientific, not sociological

The audit is scientifically defensible: hypotheses, tests, data, uncertainty bounds. It is not a sociological account; it does not explain why anyone did anything. Claims about motive, intent, or political strategy are marked as possibilities, not findings. Using this document to back extreme claims — "deliberate gerrymander" or "nothing to see here" — goes beyond what the evidence supports.

Background (if you need it)

Alberta is divided into areas called electoral districts. Each district elects one person (an MLA) to the provincial legislature. Alberta currently has 87 districts. Both 2026 proposals would change the map to 89 districts.

An independent five-person commission drew both 2026 proposals. Two commissioners were picked by the government, two by the opposition, and one by a senior judge. The commission heard public input and tabled two competing maps on March 23, 2026. The government rejected the majority option on April 16, 2026 and set up a new drafting group, mostly made up of UCP MLAs, to report back in November 2026.

Read this first

After writing the first draft, the audit checked its own work through four extra tests. All four made one or more findings less certain than they first looked.

  1. The "2 seats" number has a wider range than the first draft said. In 2,000 re-runs with slightly different assumptions, the minority map leaned UCP 89 times out of 100. In 11 it leaned the other way. The direction is probably UCP-favourable but not certain. See §3.4 for the Monte Carlo bounds.
  2. A second standard test (declination) says the minority is the least UCP-favourable of the three maps. Two tests from the same research literature point in opposite directions. See §3.4.
  3. If you use 2019 vote data instead of 2023, the direction flips entirely. Some of what the partisan tests measured is about the 2023 electorate, not the map. See §3.4.
  4. The chair's "no public support for the minority's five disputed configurations" claim is partially wrong. Three of the five actually do have public support in the 1,252 submissions searched. See §5.4.

Findings about district sizes, community splits, and unusual boundary shapes do not depend on vote data. They hold up. Findings about how the maps turn votes into seats depend on which election and which assumptions. They are less certain than the first draft implied. The overall pattern is real. The precise size of the partisan effect is not.

The short version

Both 2026 proposals satisfy Alberta's statutory requirements (district populations within ±25%, 89 divisions, tabled on time). They diverge on measurable dimensions of fairness.

Structural findings that hold up under every red-team test:

  • The minority map has a population spread that is 48% wider than the majority's. §A1
  • Calgary's NDP-leaning zones have districts 12.2% bigger than UCP-leaning zones under the minority map, versus 0.4% under the majority. §A2
  • The minority splits Airdrie into four districts and merges Cochrane with Calgary through a narrow corridor. The majority keeps both intact. §C4
  • Three minority districts (Nolan Hill-Cochrane, Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park, Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury) have shapes the commission chair flagged as engineered. §C3

Partisan-math findings that weaken under the red-team:

  • Minority efficiency gap is 1 to 3 seats more UCP-favourable than majority at the central modelling choice. 95% confidence interval crosses zero. Direction consistent in 89% of Monte Carlo samples. §3.4
  • Declination metric disagrees with efficiency gap on the direction of bias. §3.4
  • Using 2019 votes instead of 2023, the asymmetry flips sign. §3.4

Procedural concern (narrowed after submission-archive search):

  • The government's April 16 action replaces the commission drafting process with a government-majority MLA committee. Most government-controlled response to an independent provincial boundary commission among the three commonly-cited Canadian comparator cases (Quebec 1992, Ontario 1996, BC 2008). §5
  • The chair's claim that the minority's configurations had no public support is partly refuted. Two configurations (Airdrie 4-way, Nolan Hill-Cochrane) do lack documented public support. Three (Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park, Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury, Red Deer hybrids) have public support including one submission explicitly proposing Rocky Mountain House-Banff by name. §5.4

What this audit cannot tell you

  • Intent. The audit shows effects, not intentions. Don't let anyone (including the audit) claim proof of intent.
  • A single precise number for the partisan shift. A range of 1 to 3 seats in a tied election is the honest answer. Measured attribution requires 2026 boundary shapefiles, not yet released.
  • Whether the April 16 override is legal. It appears legal. Wisdom and precedent are different questions than legality.
  • What the November 2026 committee will produce. The audit can only evaluate the two commission proposals, not a map that has not yet been drafted.

Who should care, and why

If you live in Calgary and vote NDP
The minority map's districts in your neighbourhood carry about 12% more people than districts in UCP-leaning neighbourhoods. Your share of an MLA is smaller under that map. Evidence.
If you live in Airdrie or Cochrane
The minority map splits your city across multiple districts without naming one after it. The majority gives your city its own MLAs. Evidence.
If you live in rural Alberta
The minority map produces slightly smaller rural districts on average, so rural voters get slightly more MLAs per capita. That helps rural UCP voters a little; it hurts rural NDP voters a little. Evidence.
If you care about fair process regardless of party
The government is replacing an independent drafting process with a government-controlled one. Some of the boundary choices being pushed have no public support. Others have clear public support. The concern is real but narrower than the first draft of this audit implied. Evidence.

§3.4 red-team

Monte Carlo 95% CI crosses zero. N=2,000 samples varying urban weight, rural baseline, and per-hybrid jitter. Minority-majority EG asymmetry: mean −1.25 pp, 95% CI [−2.99, +0.76] pp. Direction consistency 89.3%. Under Katz, King, and Rosenblatt (2020), the appropriate reporting convention is to disclose CI bounds rather than privileging a point estimate.

Declination disagrees. 2019 = −0.034; Majority = −0.021; Minority = −0.015. By declination, minority is the least pro-UCP of the three. Warrington (2019) documents cross-metric divergence as an expected feature rather than a methodological flaw.

2019 cross-election check reverses the asymmetry. Under 2019 vote data: Majority EG +0.30%, Minority EG +0.90%, asymmetry +0.60 pp (minority less UCP-favorable). Observed asymmetry partly reflects 2023-specific voter distribution.

Chen-Rodden natural-packing context. Chen and Rodden (2013) argue urban-concentrated parties like the NDP are disadvantaged by any neutrally-drawn map because of voter geography. The 2019 baseline EG of −2.64% is partly a natural artifact. Under this framing, both 2026 maps partially correct for natural packing; the majority corrects more. This weakens the "intentional partisan choice" implication but does not affect structural findings in Sections A, C, or D.

§5 Procedural audit

The commission operated under the Electoral Boundaries Commission Act §3–5 with five members (chair nominated by the Chief Justice, two government-nominated, two opposition-nominated). The April 16, 2026 government action established a UCP-majority MLA committee (chaired by Brandon Lunty) to oversee a new drafting panel reporting November 2, 2026. Within the Reference re Provincial Electoral Boundaries (Saskatchewan) [1991] 2 SCR 158 "effective representation" framework, this constitutes a mid-cycle replacement of the independent drafting process, not just an amendment to its output.

Comparator cases: Quebec 1992 (narrow amendments to commission output; drafting process retained), Ontario 1996 (adopted federal-commission boundaries; no government-drafting), British Columbia 2008 (legislated retention of extra Northern seats; commission drafting retained). All three amended outputs through regular legislation. Alberta April 16 goes further by replacing the drafting process itself. Courtney (2001) provides the authoritative treatment of Canadian independent-commission practice; Pal (2015, 2019) applies contemporary quantitative gerrymandering analysis to Canadian cases within the Charter framework.

§7 Synthesis

Six independent dimensions of evidence point in the same direction on the minority-to-majority comparison. None individually crosses a statistical significance threshold. Together, the directional consistency across structural (population, Calgary zone, community splits, visible anomalies) and procedural dimensions supports a finding of directionally-consistent partisan asymmetry at a sub-threshold magnitude. The partisan-math magnitude claim is qualified by Monte Carlo CI bounds, cross-metric disagreement, and cross-election direction sensitivity. The procedural critique is narrowed by the submission-archive finding to focus on configurations without documented public support.

Data and methods

AI pipeline: Claude Opus 4.7 1M (Max subscription) through Claude Code CLI. Two background sub-agents: (1) submission archive keyword search across 27 batch PDFs; (2) shapefile and census data acquisition.

Integrity framework: stage-based falsifiability gates G0–G5 (v1.2 prompt) plus red-team gates RT1–RT6 (Monte Carlo CI, cross-metric agreement, cross-election stability, structural-vs-vote-based separation, independent test selection, assumption inventory) plus publication gates PR1–PR4 (voice, readability, reproducibility manifest, changelog).
