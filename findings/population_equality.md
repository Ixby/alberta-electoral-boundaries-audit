# Section A — Population Equality

**Sanity-check preamble.** Under a neutral redistribution, variance should be modest and symmetric. EDs above +10% and below −10% from the provincial average should appear in comparable numbers across both 2026 proposals. If one proposal has markedly wider dispersion, or a directional geographic asymmetry (e.g., urban ridings of one political lean systematically larger), that is a detectable population-equality signal. s.15(2) protected ridings should independently satisfy 3 of 5 statutory criteria — if not, their boundaries have plausibly been drawn *to* qualify.

The expected pattern going in: if the B1–B4 partisan-bias finding (minority shifts 2–3 pp toward UCP) has a population-equality correlate, we should see minority NDP-leaning districts systematically larger than UCP-leaning ones. The numbers confirm exactly this.

## A1 — Variance Distribution

Provincial average is **54,929** per district under both 2026 maps (4,888,723 and 4,888,773 respectively; the 50-person gap is rounding in the commission's own totals, not an error). 2019-era population data is not present in the working bundle; historical deviations for 2019 boundaries were within ±25% at the time of the 2017 commission's drawing, so this section focuses on the two 2026 proposals.

| N districts                            | 89            | 89            |
| Mean population                        | 54,929        | 54,930        |
| Median population                      | 55,791        | 55,713        |
| Standard deviation                     | 5,301         | 6,533         |
| Mean absolute deviation from 54,929    | **3,180**     | **4,707**     |
| Max positive deviation                 | +14.28%       | **+24.06%**   |
| Max negative deviation                 | −47.72%       | −45.36%       |
| EDs above +10%                         | 5             | **15**        |
| EDs above +15%                         | 0             | **5**         |
| EDs above +20%                         | 0             | **3**         |

| EDs below −15%                         | 4             | 5             |
| EDs below −20%                         | 4             | 4             |

**Finding.** The minority proposal has **48% more** mean-absolute-deviation from the provincial average than the majority (4,707 vs 3,180). The tails are asymmetric: the minority has 15 ridings above +10% versus the majority's 5 (3× more), and 5 ridings above +15% versus zero. The single largest positive deviation in the minority map (+24.06%) sits within 1 percentage point of the statutory ±25% cap. Neither map crosses the cap. Both maps use all three of their s.15(2) slots for the maximum permitted sub-(−25%) districts.

A wider dispersion is not by itself a gerrymandering signal — it is a population-equality signal. Whether the dispersion *correlates with partisan geography* is what A2 tests.

## A2 — Geographic Asymmetry (Calgary NE/central vs S/W)

Calgary EDs are classified into two geographic zones corresponding to the city's historical partisan geography: NE/central (traditionally NDP-competitive or NDP-won) vs S/W (traditionally UCP-dominant). The question: under each proposal, is one zone systematically more populous than the other? A systematically larger NDP-leaning zone means more NDP voters packed per district — fewer NDP districts — the classic packing signature.

All 28 majority Calgary EDs and all 29 minority Calgary EDs classified with no residuals.

| Zone                             | Majority (n / mean pop) | Minority (n / mean pop) |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Calgary NE/central               | 17 / **56,460**         | 17 / **61,225**         |
| Calgary S/W                      | 11 / 56,255             | 12 / 54,569             |
| Gap (NE/central − S/W)           | **+205 (+0.36%)**       | **+6,656 (+12.20%)**    |

**Finding.** Under the majority proposal, Calgary NE/central and S/W are within 0.4% of each other's mean population — indistinguishable. Under the minority proposal, Calgary NE/central ridings are **12.2% larger** on average than their S/W counterparts. That gap is ~6,656 people per riding, implied over 17 NE/central districts, absorbing roughly 113,000 "extra" voters that would otherwise need to be represented in additional NDP-leaning ridings. This is directionally consistent with the B1–B4 finding of a 2–3 pp minority shift toward UCP, and gives a mechanism (packing) for the shift that is visible at the population level, before any vote attribution.

## A2b — Calgary / Edmonton / Rest-of-Province breakdown

| Region      | Majority (n / mean)   | Minority (n / mean)   |
| ----------- | --------------------- | --------------------- |
| Calgary     | 28 / 56,379           | 29 / 58,470           |
| Edmonton    | 21 / 58,041           | 22 / 58,198           |
| Rest        | 40 / 52,281           | 38 / **50,336**       |

**Finding.** Both maps keep Edmonton's mean population close (58,041 vs 58,198). Calgary runs slightly higher in the minority (58,470 vs 56,379). The most notable regional move is that the minority's rest-of-province mean (**50,336**) is materially lower than the majority's (52,281) — a 3.9% difference. Smaller rural districts means proportionally **more rural seats** for the same provincial population. Given rural Alberta's 70/30 UCP lean documented in the 2023 carry-forward, smaller rural districts is a **rural-overrepresentation signal** favoring UCP. The majority proposal moves in the same direction but less.

## A3 — s.15(2) Eligibility Audit

The Electoral Boundaries Commission Act §15(2) permits a district up to −50% variance (vs the standard ±25%) if it meets at least 3 of the following 5 criteria independently of the boundary drawing:

(a) area > 20,000 km²
(b) > 150 km from the Legislature Building by most direct highway (distance to nearest ED boundary)
(c) no town with population exceeding 8,000 in the district (Municipality of Crowsnest Pass excluded per §15(3))
(d) contains an Indian reserve or a Métis settlement (presence test, not a demographic threshold)
(e) a portion of the ED boundary is coterminous with a provincial boundary

Each proposal invokes s.15(2) for three ridings. Criteria evaluated against EBC Final Report (March 2026), 2021 census data, Natural Resources Canada atlas, and Treaty boundary maps.

| Central Peace-Notley (majority) | −47.7% | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (Peace River ~6,500) | ✓ | ✓ (BC) | **5/5** | PASS |
| Lesser Slave Lake (majority) | −45.4% | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (Slave Lake ~7,000) | ✓ (14 communities) | ✗ | **4/5** | PASS |

All six invocations are statutorily eligible. Verdict for the minority's Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park requires note:

**Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park (minority, 5/5 — engineered).** The commission chair described this boundary as "artificially extending its boundary to the province's western border with British Columbia (taking part of Banff National Park, where no one lives), which is a bad faith effort to ensure it can be protected under s. 15(2)" (EBC Final Report, p. 10). The statutory test is met: criteria (a) and (e) both pass. But both pass *only because* the boundary was extended through uninhabited national park territory. Absent the NP extension the area is ~15,000 km² (failing (a)) and there is no BC border (failing (e)). The commission chair's own characterisation is preserved here because it is the primary-source description of the engineering mechanism — not an inference by this audit. The RMH area independently satisfies (b), (c), and (d) regardless of the NP extension (3/5 minimum without it). The boundary extension adds two additional criteria passes but no represented community.

### Symmetric note

Both maps have three s.15(2) invocations that pass the 3/5 statutory test. The count is a wash. The character diverges: the majority's Canmore-Banff passes on genuine criteria (b), (d), (e) despite the small area; the minority's Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park passes all five criteria but two of those passes (area and border) depend on an extension through uninhabited park territory that the commission chair called "bad faith." No invocation fails the statutory threshold on either map.

## Symmetric Assessment

| Test                                  | Majority 2026 | Minority 2026 | Observation                                                |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------- | ------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| A1: MAD from provincial avg           | 3,180         | **4,707**     | Minority 48% wider variance                                |

**Population-equality finding.** On pure headcount terms both maps are statutorily permissible (no ED over ±25% except s.15(2) invocations, which both maps use up to the legal maximum of 3). On fairness terms the two maps diverge sharply. The majority proposal has tight, roughly neutral dispersion with one judgment-call s.15(2). The minority proposal has wider dispersion concentrated in a pattern — NE/central Calgary packed ~12% larger than S/W Calgary, rural mean population dropped below the majority's — that systematically over-represents UCP-leaning geography and under-represents NDP-leaning urban geography. This is a population-equality correlate of the B1–B4 partisan-bias asymmetry.
