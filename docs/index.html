Alberta Electoral Boundary Audit

An independent look at Alberta's two 2026 riding maps — using 1,010,000 computer-drawn comparison maps to see if the results are normal or unusual.

What happened

Alberta has 89 seats in its legislature. A five-person commission was appointed to draw the riding boundaries — the lines that determine which communities each seat represents. The commission split: three members produced one map, and two members produced a different one. On April 16, 2026, the provincial government rejected both and handed the job to a five-MLA committee, three of whom are from the governing United Conservative Party (UCP). This audit compared the two commission maps using the same tests, applied equally to both.

What is a riding? A riding (also called an electoral district) is the area you share with your MLA. Every riding elects one person to the legislature. Where the boundary lines go matters: move a few streets one way and a neighbourhood ends up in a rural riding. Move them the other way and it stays with other city residents. Those choices can change how many seats each party wins — even without changing a single vote.

Three key numbers

Seats at a tied vote
5 seats
If the vote split exactly 50-50, the minority map gives the NDP 43 seats and the majority map gives them 48 — a 5-seat swing, without changing a single ballot.
Wasted vote gap
41×
The minority map wastes roughly 36,000 more NDP votes than UCP votes. The majority map's gap is only ~881. The minority map's gap is 41 times larger.
Riding size spread
48%
The minority map's ridings vary more in population — 4,707 people off average versus 3,180 under the majority map. More variation means votes don't count equally.
A computer program drew 1,010,000 random Alberta maps, all following the same rules the commission had to follow. The majority map's seat result was normal — most random maps look similar. The minority map's result was extreme: fewer than 100 of the 1,010,000 random maps produced a seat split that far in favour of the UCP at a tied vote.

What this does not say

It does not call either map a "gerrymander." "Gerrymander" is an American legal term for a specific kind of unconstitutional map. Canada does not have that law. In Canada, the legal question is whether voters have "effective representation" under the Charter of Rights and Freedoms and whether the commission followed the Electoral Boundaries Commission Act. That question belongs to courts and legislators — not this audit.

It does not say anyone did this on purpose. Statistics show what a map does, not what the people who drew it intended. There is no way to prove intent from numbers alone.

What about urban concentration? NDP voters are concentrated in city cores. Even a perfectly neutral map tends to give the NDP a small disadvantage, because votes packed into a few ridings win big there but leave nothing for surrounding ridings. The 1,010,000 random maps in the simulation were drawn using actual Alberta geography — this is already accounted for. The minority map is still extreme compared to those neutral draws. The difference cannot be explained by geography alone.

How the analysis was done

The maps

Official Elections Alberta riding maps in digital format, received May 6, 2026. The 2023 election results were matched to each riding by checking which riding each polling station fell inside.

The computer simulation

A program called GerryChain drew 1,010,000 new riding maps for Alberta, following the same population rules the commission had to follow. This gives a baseline for what a "normal" Alberta map looks like. The commission maps are then compared against that baseline. Four independent runs were done to make sure the results were consistent.

Pre-registration

All tests were written down and publicly filed before the results were examined. This prevents cherry-picking results after the fact. Registration IDs: OSF:6pt83, AsPredicted:#289,469, AsPredicted:#289,451.
