# v0.1 — 91-Seat Proposal: Preliminary Analysis

> **DISCLAIMER — READ FIRST.**
> As of the analysis date (2026-04-22) **no structured 91-seat boundary
> proposal has been released**. The 91-seat figure originates as an addendum
> recommendation by Commission Chair Dallas Miller and has been adopted as the
> mandate of a newly-struck Alberta Legislative select committee (chaired by
> UCP MLA Brandon Lunty). That committee is to report by **Nov 2, 2026**; an
> independent advisory panel will advise on how to divide Alberta into 91
> divisions.
>
> Everything in this document beyond the cited news reporting is inference,
> explicitly labelled **SPECULATION** or with a confidence band
> (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW). The main audit should not publish any
> gerrymander-quantification claim on the 91-seat version until an actual
> proposed map with shapefiles or population tables is tabled.

## 2. What is NOT known

- **No public 91-seat map.** Neither the government, the Lunty committee, nor
  any media outlet has released a candidate 91-seat ED layout.
- **No geographic allocation announced.** Where the 2 additional seats land
  (Calgary / Edmonton / rural; or different zone-split) is undecided.
- **No partisan projection on 91 seats.** factsmtr's UCP 48 / NDP 41 figure
  (F8) is *89-seat majority report only*; no comparable estimate exists for
  91.
- **No methodology note.** The advisory panel's mandate (population tolerance,
  s15(2) rules, communities-of-interest tests) has not been published.

## 3. Inference framework — what 91 seats would plausibly do

> Every item in this section is an **inference** from structural facts.
> Confidence labels apply per-item.

### 3.1 The 2 extra seats would almost certainly not land in rural Alberta

**Confidence: HIGH.** The premier's rationale (F7) is that the commission
"did not want to lose two rural ridings" — i.e. 91 seats preserves rural
ridings that 89 seats would consolidate. That means **the two extra seats
relative to the 89-seat majority report are `rural-preserved` seats, not new
seats for Calgary/Edmonton**. The 89-seat majority report already gave
Calgary +2 and Edmonton +1; a 91-seat version most likely keeps those urban
additions and adds back the two rural seats that were consolidated.

Alternative readings:
- **LOW confidence alt.** If the advisory panel re-opens the full allocation,
  the 2 extra seats could go anywhere. But the premier's framing and the
  Miller addendum's rationale make this unlikely.

### 3.2 Effect on rural overrepresentation

**Confidence: MEDIUM–HIGH.** If the two extra seats are rural-preserved
relative to the 89-seat majority map, then the rural average population drops
(more rural seats, same rural population). That **amplifies** rural
overrepresentation — the exact axis the audit has flagged as a concern in
both the 89-seat majority and minority maps. Mechanically:

- Majority (89): 40 rural seats at mean 52,281 pop.
- Minority (89): 38 rural seats at mean 50,336 pop. (−3.72% vs majority; see
  `rural_gap_findings.md` for drill-down).
- 91-seat scenario, **SPECULATION label, MEDIUM confidence**: ~42 rural seats
  with the same total rural population would move the rural mean to
  approximately 49,790 — i.e. further under the provincial 54,929 mean and
  **worse than the minority map** on this axis.

### 3.3 Effect on Calgary zone-gap

**Confidence: MEDIUM.** The audit's Calgary zone-gap finding (Miller's own
language from F6 context: 15 central/NE Calgary EDs ~11% above provincial
average, 14 south/west EDs ~2% above) is about *intra-Calgary imbalance*, not
Calgary-vs-rest. Adding two rural seats without re-drawing Calgary leaves the
Calgary zone-gap **essentially unchanged** at ~9 pp between northeast and
southwest. If the advisory panel reopens Calgary wards, the direction is
genuinely undetermined.

### 3.4 Effect on B2 efficiency gap / 95% CI

**Confidence: LOW — flagged as SPECULATION.** Without an actual map the
efficiency gap cannot be recomputed. Directional reasoning only:

- The audit's B2 efficiency gap is bounded by (a) vote distribution
  asymmetries across ridings and (b) the number of seats. Adding 2 rural
  UCP-packed seats (see §3.2 and the rural-gap finding that rural under-pop
  is concentrated in ~68–70% UCP territory in both 89-seat maps) would
  **increase wasted NDP votes outside those seats** (urban NDP surplus
  relative to seats) and **increase wasted UCP votes inside those seats**
  (deep-UCP seats waste UCP surplus above 50%+1). The net direction on the
  efficiency gap depends on which effect dominates.
- **SPECULATION — LOW confidence.** A plausible first-order guess: adding
  rural UCP-packed seats nudges efficiency gap in the UCP-favour direction,
  because Alberta's urban NDP concentrations are large enough that adding
  seats anywhere other than dense NDP urban cores translates UCP voters more
  efficiently. The 95% CI would shift along with the point estimate; it
  would not obviously narrow or widen on n=91 vs n=89 (both large enough that
  binomial CI dominance over the gerrymander signal is small).
- **Do not publish a quantitative efficiency-gap claim for the 91-seat
  scenario until a map exists.** The direction argument above is sign-only.

### 3.5 Effect on the 3.9% rural-mean gap finding

**Confidence: MEDIUM.** The 3.9% gap is between the *two 89-seat maps*
(majority vs minority). A 91-seat map is a *third* map; it does not "change"
the 3.9% gap, it introduces a new data point. If the 91-seat version uses
more s15(2) seats (which seems likely given Miller's "don't lose rural
ridings" framing), its rural mean will be **below both** 89-seat versions —
i.e. the 91-seat map is likely the most rural-overweighted of the three.

## 4. Procedural / democratic-legitimacy flags

These are not statistical findings but procedural observations relevant to
the audit's §D (Procedural) section:

1. **CONFIRMED.** The 91-seat workstream is **government-led via a legislative
   committee chaired by a government MLA**, not an independent commission.
   This is a qualitative break from the process that produced the 89-seat
   report. (F2, F3)
2. **CONFIRMED.** Elections Alberta has publicly flagged a timeline risk
   (F5): <18 months to fall 2027 general election with a Nov 2, 2026
   committee-report deadline means implementation within ~10–12 months of
   the election. This is tighter than Elections Alberta's preferred
   threshold.
3. **MEDIUM confidence.** The 91-seat mandate originates in a conditional
   addendum by the commission chair, not in a majority finding of the
   independent commission. Styling it as "implementing the commission's
   recommendation" overstates its procedural provenance.

## 5. Recommended stance for the main audit

- **Include** a section acknowledging the 91-seat process exists and
  summarising F1–F9. Cite all sources with dates.
- **Do not** publish any quantitative gerrymander metric (LH index,
  efficiency gap, zone-gap, rural mean) for the 91-seat scenario until a
  proposed map is released.
- **Do** publish the directional reasoning in §3.1 and §3.2 with confidence
  labels intact — specifically, the falsifiable prediction that a 91-seat
  map, built on Miller's "preserve two rural ridings" framing, will have a
  **lower rural mean population than the minority 89-seat map (50,336)**
  and will worsen rural overrepresentation relative to either 89-seat map.
- **Flag** the procedural break (§4) in the audit's procedural section.
- **Commit** to updating the audit with a proper 91-seat quantitative run
  once the committee or advisory panel releases maps or a population table
  (expected on or before 2026-11-02).
