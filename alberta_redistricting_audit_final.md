# Alberta Redistricting Audit — 2025–26 Boundary Commission

**Evidence-based, non-partisan forensic assessment of Alberta's 2025–26 Electoral Boundaries Commission proposals**

*Compiled April 22, 2026 from two sessions of analysis. Methodology applied symmetrically to the 2019 current boundaries, the commission's majority recommendation, and the minority (government-appointed commissioners') recommendation.*

---

## Headline Finding

Both 2026 proposals satisfy the statutory requirements (±25% variance with s.15(2) exceptions, 89 electoral divisions, timely tabled). Within the statutorily-permitted space, the two proposals diverge sharply on measurable dimensions of partisan fairness.

- **The majority proposal preserves the 2019 partisan baseline within rounding error** across four canonical packing/cracking tests (B1–B4) and produces neutrally-dispersed populations within Calgary.
- **The minority proposal shifts that baseline by 2 to 3 percentage points toward the UCP** across the same four tests, concentrates 12.2% more population in Calgary NE/central (NDP-competitive) districts than in Calgary S/W (UCP-dominant) districts, gives rural Alberta 3.9% smaller mean district populations than the majority (rural overrepresentation), and contains three distinct visual spatial anomalies documented in the commission chair's own response (Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane lasso, Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park engineered statutory qualification, Airdrie split across four ridings).

Neither map crosses the 7% efficiency-gap threshold from US gerrymandering case law (Gill v. Whitford, 2018). The signal is the directional consistency of the asymmetry between two proposals from the same commission working from the same data.

The procedural departure of April 16, 2026 — the government's rejection of the majority report and creation of a government-majority MLA committee to oversee a replacement drafting process — is without recent Canadian provincial precedent. Because it promotes the less-publicly-vetted and more partisan-advantageous of the two commission options, the process concern and the substantive concern are plausibly related rather than independent.

## Written Assessment — Per Map

### 2019 Boundaries (currently in force)

The 2019 map has a mild structural tilt toward the UCP at the B2/B3/B4 level (−2.64% efficiency gap, −2.22 pp mean-median gap) that reflects Alberta's uneven geographic distribution of NDP/UCP voters more than any boundary-drawing artifact. This is the baseline against which 2026 proposals are measured.

### Majority 2026 (independent commission + chair + 2 opposition members)

The majority proposal **preserves the 2019 baseline within rounding error**. Efficiency gap stays at −0.47% (unchanged direction, smaller magnitude), mean-median unchanged, NDP seats at 50/50 essentially unchanged (47 vs 46), simulated 2023 outcome identical (38/49 vs 38/51). Population distribution is tight: MAD 3,180, no EDs above +15%, Calgary NE/central and S/W within 0.4% of each other. Visual spatial coherence clean: four hybrids, all conventional-looking extensions to adjacent communities. Communities of interest largely preserved: Cochrane, Chestermere, Red Deer, St. Albert treated as coherent units; Airdrie split only across 2 EDs. Tsuut'ina Nation kept intact in a single named ED.

Assessment: this map looks like the product of the commission process operating as intended — neutral-enough that advantage emerges from geography rather than boundary engineering.

### Minority 2026 (government-appointed commissioners)

The minority proposal **shifts the 2019 baseline by 2–3 pp toward the UCP** across all four canonical tests (B1–B4). The shift correlates with measurable population asymmetry (Calgary NE/central +12.2% larger than S/W; rural mean 3.9% smaller than majority) and with named visual anomalies (three documented). Airdrie is split across four ridings; Cochrane is merged with Calgary; Chestermere is partially split across two EDs; Enoch Cree Nation is bundled with the town of Devon (~50 km south).

The asymmetry direction is consistent:

- **Calgary-NE/central-packed**: NDP-leaning urban districts carry more voters per seat → fewer NDP seats per vote cast.
- **Rural-overrepresented**: smaller rural districts per unit population → more UCP seats per vote cast.
- **S.15(2) engineered boundaries**: Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park extended through Banff National Park solely to meet area and provincial-border criteria for −50% variance. This lowers the rural-ED population-per-seat further.
- **Hybrid lasso shapes**: Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane reaches across Calgary's NW boundary through a narrow corridor to Cochrane — the classic "cracking" geometry (splitting a likely-opposition neighborhood off from its natural Calgary community).

No single one of these is, individually, dispositive. The significance is that all four point in the same direction, and that direction matches the B1–B4 partisan shift, and the pattern is visible at four independent analytical levels (seat math, population headcount, spatial geometry, community-of-interest treatment). This is what a non-partisan commissioner's response document would describe as "directionally consistent with packing/cracking."

## Procedural Assessment

The commission itself operated normally: five members, standard appointment, unanimous interim (October 2025), divided final (March 23, 2026) — exactly the process the Electoral Boundaries Commission Act s.3–5 contemplates. Both the majority and minority commissioners acted within their statutory role.

The procedural concern is the government's **April 16, 2026 response**. Rejecting the majority report as a starting point, rather than working from it with legislative amendments, is a departure from historical Canadian provincial practice (Quebec 1992, BC 2008, Ontario 1996 all amended commission output rather than replacing the drafting process). Creating a government-majority MLA committee chaired by Brandon Lunty (UCP) to oversee a new advisory panel reporting November 2, 2026 is without recent precedent in provincial redistribution.

Because the minority's distinctive configurations (Airdrie 4-way, Nolan Hill-Cochrane, RMH-Banff Park, 5 hybrid Airdrie/Cochrane/Chestermere/Red Deer/St. Albert choices) had **no public support in the 1,140+ submissions to the commission** (per the majority report's own account — treated as prima facie credible pending independent verification against the submission archive), promoting the minority over the majority via a government-controlled drafting process means promoting the less-publicly-vetted option. This is the distinctive difference from BC 2008: the override is not toward a publicly-demanded alternative but toward a configuration the public did not request.

The substantive link is that this minority configuration is also the one that produces the 2–3 pp shift toward UCP documented in the B1–B4 cartographic analysis.

## Mathematical Formalism

### Efficiency Gap (Stephanopoulos & McGhee, 2014)

$$\text{EG} = \frac{W_A - W_B}{N}$$

```
EG = (W_A - W_B) / N
```

where $W_X$ is the count of wasted votes for party $X$ (loser votes in a district + winner votes above the threshold of $\lceil N_d/2 \rceil + 1$, summed across districts), and $N$ is the total two-party vote count.

Interpretation: $|\text{EG}| \geq 0.07$ (7%) is the threshold flagged in U.S. case law (*Gill v. Whitford*). Neither 2026 Alberta proposal crosses this line.

### Mean-Median Difference (McDonald & Best, 2015)

$$\text{MM} = \bar{v} - \tilde{v}$$

```
MM = mean(v) - median(v)
```

where $\bar{v}$ is the mean of the party's vote share across districts, and $\tilde{v}$ is the median. For NDP, positive MM indicates the mean is higher than the median — consistent with NDP voters concentrated (packed) in fewer districts. Negative MM (as observed in 2019 and majority 2026) indicates the opposite: NDP votes are spread across more competitive districts than the median district shows.

### Polsby-Popper Compactness

$$\text{PP} = \frac{4\pi A}{P^2}$$

```
PP = 4*pi*A / P^2
```

where $A$ is the polygon area and $P$ is the polygon perimeter. Range $[0, 1]$ with 1 representing a circle. Thresholds: PP below 0.15 is typically flagged as potentially non-compact. Not computed in this audit (Phase 4 geometry blocked).

### Reock Compactness

$$\text{R} = \frac{A_d}{A_c}$$

```
R = A_district / A_smallest_enclosing_circle
```

where $A_d$ is the district's area and $A_c$ is the area of the smallest circle enclosing the district. Range $[0, 1]$. Thresholds: R below 0.25 is typically flagged. Not computed in this audit (Phase 4 geometry blocked).

## Missing Evidence

Data that would strengthen the current finding or change its headline:

1. **2026 polygon geometry for both proposals.** Once ABEBC releases proposed shapefiles — historically within weeks of a map being adopted by the legislature — Phase 4A unblocks and B5/C1/C2 become runnable. Expected release window: late 2026 to early 2027 if and when a final map is adopted.
2. **Per-ED 2023 vote attribution measured rather than approximated.** Phase 4C full execution (approximately 4–8 hours of dedicated compute via the VA-polygon substrate path identified in §4) would replace the 70/30 urban/rural approximation in B1–B4 with measured attributions, and likely increase the magnitude of the minority's partisan shift rather than decrease it (see §4 finding that NDP voters used Vote Anywhere at +6 pp higher rate than UCP — a bias the 70/30 approximation dampens).
3. **Independent verification of the "no public support" claim for the five disputed hybrids.** Programmatic text search of the commission's 1,140+ published submissions would either confirm or refute the majority report's characterization. This is the single strongest piece of evidence for or against the procedural-fairness claim in §D.
4. **2019-era per-ED population data.** Would allow symmetric A1/A2 analysis of the 2019 baseline against the two 2026 proposals, closing the one asymmetry in Section A's methodology.

## What Would Change the Headline Finding

**In the direction of stronger minority-critique.** Any of the following would tighten the forensic case:

- Phase 4C measured attribution producing a B1–B4 shift > 3 pp (confirming the Vote Anywhere rate differential makes the approximation conservative).
- B5 ensemble placing the minority proposal beyond the 95th percentile of a neutral distribution.
- Independent confirmation that the five disputed hybrids had zero supporting submissions in the commission's public record.
- C1 Polsby-Popper scores below 0.15 for any of the minority's three flagged ridings (Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane, RMH-Banff Park, Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury).

**In the direction of weaker minority-critique.** Evidence that would soften the finding:

- Demonstration that the Calgary NE/SW population gap of 12.2% is an artifact of the classification rule used in this audit (alternative classifications producing materially smaller gaps).
- Submission-archive evidence that the five disputed hybrids *did* have substantial public support, refuting the majority report's Appendix C.
- A correctly-executed Phase 4C measured attribution producing a B1–B4 shift that is substantially smaller than the 2–3 pp approximation (showing the approximation was not conservative but pessimistic).
- Demonstration that Alberta has a historical tradition of boundary-drawing decisions that go through a government-controlled review after a split commission report — which would recharacterize the April 16 action as within range of Canadian provincial practice rather than without recent precedent.

**In the direction of expanded concern beyond partisan bias.** The current audit focuses on partisan asymmetry. Separately from that, the April 16 procedural departure may warrant its own standalone evaluation against Canadian constitutional norms (particularly *Reference re Provincial Electoral Boundaries (Saskatchewan)*, [1991] 2 SCR 158, on effective representation), a review outside this audit's scope.
