## RED HAT — Emotional Responses to the Strategic Situation

### On the absence of institutional allies
The audit is published. It is on GitHub. It is methodologically sophisticated. And as of writing, there is no documented uptake — no news story, no academic co-author, no NDP press release, no CCLA statement. This situation feels like a tree falling in a forest. The work is real. Whether it has an audience yet is a different question.

### On the timing advantage
There is something genuinely exciting about having a pre-registered 17-test grid ready and waiting for the November 2026 committee map. The audit's frame is already set. When the map drops, the analysis runs. Within 72 hours, a scorecard exists. That's a faster, more rigorous response than any political party or advocacy organization could produce. The timing advantage is real and feels like the project's strongest strategic asset.

### On the risk of partisan capture
The NDP has the most incentive to amplify the audit. That is also the audit's greatest reputational risk. If the audit becomes associated with NDP partisanship, government critics will dismiss it regardless of its methodological quality. The author is described as "no political party affiliation" and this matters. Letting the NDP lead with audit findings would be a strategic error.

### On Duane Bratt as the key relationship
Duane Bratt is a political science professor at MRU — the same institution as the author — with a public profile and media credibility on Alberta politics. A positive engagement from him represents a path to academic legitimacy, media access, and peer review credibility simultaneously. The uncertainty about whether he has responded is uncomfortable. This is the highest-priority relationship.

### On being a student author
There is a real risk that journalists, politicians, and academics will discount the audit because it was produced by a student. This is an unfair but predictable reaction. The audit needs at least one credentialed academic to engage with it substantively before November 2026. Without that, it may be dismissed as undergraduate work regardless of its quality.

### On the Enoch Cree Nation dimension
The finding that the minority map pairs Enoch Cree Nation with distant Devon rather than adjacent Edmonton is the dimension that could attract the broadest coalition of concern. Indigenous electoral representation is a constitutional issue with far more legal traction than partisan efficiency gap arguments. The fact that this dimension is underdeveloped in the current report feels like a missed strategic opportunity.

## BLACK HAT — Strategic Risks and Attack Surfaces

### Risk 1: Partisan capture renders the audit dismissible
The moment the audit is quoted in an NDP press release, the UCP can dismiss it as opposition research. The audit's independence claim rests on the author's stated non-affiliation. If the NDP adopts the audit before academic validation occurs, the research is rhetorically captured regardless of its quality. This risk is higher than any methodological weakness.

### Risk 2: The government simply ignores it
The Lunty committee has no obligation to respond to external audits. The government's track record — rejecting the independent commission majority itself — suggests that evidence of procedural or statistical irregularity will not change its course of action. The audit may produce a precise, pre-registered scorecard of November 2026 findings that are then tabled in a legislature where the government has a majority and does nothing.

### Risk 3: The methodology becomes the story instead of the findings
If a single academic statistician publicly criticizes the family-wise error rate problem or the E2 reformulation, the news cycle will shift to "audit has methodological flaws" rather than "minority map shows five structural departures from commission methodology." The CRITICAL issues were caught and corrected, but the HIGH issues remain. Any credentialed critic who reads the paper carefully will find genuine ammunition.

### Risk 4: The November 2026 map is clean
If the Lunty committee produces a 91-seat map that passes all 17 pre-registered tests, the audit's most credible contribution — the prospective pre-registration — will produce a finding of no anomaly. This is scientifically the correct outcome if the map is clean. But it leaves the retrospective audit's more complex partisan-bias findings as the primary record, without the prospective confirmation that would have given them credibility.

### Risk 5: The 338Canada reversal gets seized by critics
The April 2026 polling shows +1 NDP seat under the minority map — the opposite direction from the EG finding. A government critic who understands the audit well enough can argue: "Your own analysis shows the minority map produces NDP seats under current polling. How is that a UCP-favorable gerrymander?" The audit's answer (vote-distribution dependence; 2023 election substrate vs 2026 polling) is technically correct but complex to explain under media pressure.

### Risk 6: Time pressure before November 2026
The Bonferroni correction, the 77-snapshot sensitivity, the 2019-seeded MCMC ensemble, and the methods paper are all open. If November arrives without these completed, the pre-registered grid will run on a methodology that still has documented HIGH-level weaknesses. A critic can use those weaknesses to dismiss the November scorecard even if it finds anomalies.

### Risk 7: Solo author sustainability
Building, maintaining, and defending a 234-node DAG, eight-stage geometry pipeline, and pre-registered analysis framework as a single person while completing a university degree is not sustainable indefinitely. If the author reaches capacity before November, the pre-registration commitment cannot be fulfilled at the quality level the framework requires.

### Risk 8: Legal action threat
If the audit is widely disseminated and the minority commissioners feel their reputations are damaged, there is a non-zero risk of legal pressure. The audit consistently avoids using the word "gerrymander" as a verdict and correctly uses "gerrymander checklist" and statistical findings only. This is the right legal posture. But if media amplification forces the findings into popular shorthand ("audit finds gerrymander"), the legal risk increases regardless of what the audit actually says.

## YELLOW HAT — Strategic Opportunities and Strengths

### The 72-hour pre-registered scorecard is a genuine competitive advantage
No political party, media organization, or existing watchdog can produce a pre-registered, 17-test, methodology-documented analysis of a redistricting map in 72 hours. The audit has built this capacity. When the committee map drops on November 2, 2026, the audit's scorecard will be the first rigorous public analysis available. That timing advantage is a significant public-interest contribution regardless of what the scorecard says.

### The public submission analysis creates a non-partisan entry point
The finding that 14 recovered submissions support at least one minority-contested configuration challenges the commission chair's "no public support" claim. This is a factual finding with no partisan interpretation required — it simply says public input was mischaracterized. This is an easier story for journalists to cover and for a broader audience to care about than EG percentile ranks.

### The Act amendment proposal gives politicians something constructive to do with the findings
The specific legislative recommendations — 90-day public comment minimum, paired population tables, written deviation explanations — give opposition MLAs a bill to write. Converting an audit finding into specific legislative text gives the research a direct political pathway that doesn't require the government to admit wrongdoing.

### The Enoch Cree Nation finding has the broadest coalition potential
Indigenous electoral representation intersects with s.35 constitutional rights, federal Crown obligations, and UNDRIP. A finding about how the minority map treats Enoch Cree Nation's community-of-interest representation connects to a much larger national conversation than partisan efficiency gaps. Engaging an Indigenous legal scholar or First Nations organization as co-reviewer on this section would simultaneously strengthen the analysis and broaden the coalition.

### Academic journals are reachable before November 2026
The Canadian Journal of Political Science and Election Law Journal have publication timelines of 3–6 months from submission to decision. A submission in June or July 2026 could have a decision (even a "revise and resubmit") before November. Even a preprint on SSRN with a stated journal submission establishes academic legitimacy ahead of the committee map.

### The methods paper is publishable regardless of the audit's conclusions
A paper titled "Constructing Provincial Electoral Geometries Without Official Shapefiles: A Staged Provisional Approach" has academic merit independent of the Alberta redistricting controversy. It solves a genuine methodological problem. It would be cited by future redistricting researchers in every Canadian province. It does not require taking a position on whether the minority map is a gerrymander. This is publishable in statistical methods journals with no political science controversy.

### The open-source reproducibility creates a coalition of engaged researchers
The public GitHub repository is an invitation for independent researchers to replicate or extend the work. Actively promoting the repository — through political science, statistics, and computer science networks — creates a community of people with standing knowledge of the methodology. If a critic attacks the analysis, the community can respond.

### The dependency DAG allows surgical response to any specific attack
If a critic argues that the vote-attribution method is wrong, the dependency DAG shows exactly which findings are downstream of that assumption and which are not. This allows a precise, non-defensive response: "Your objection, if valid, affects findings X and Y. Findings A, B, C, and D are vote-attribution-independent and unaffected." This is a uniquely powerful rhetorical tool that most research does not have.

## GREEN HAT — Strategic Possibilities and Creative Paths

### The counter-map is the most powerful single deliverable before November
If Issue #14 (produce a counter-map achieving minority objectives with majority-level municipal anchoring) is completed, it becomes the audit's strongest strategic asset. The counter-map can be presented to media, politicians, and academics with a simple framing: "We showed it was possible to achieve the same representational goals without these anomalies. This is what those boundaries could have looked like." No statistical literacy required. The anomaly is visible.

### Commission the S.15(2) analysis as a standalone Indigenous rights document
The finding that the minority map's Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park extension appears engineered to meet the s.15(2) remote-district population exception has implications beyond partisan bias. This could be framed as a standalone document focused on the integrity of the s.15(2) exception — how it is being used, whether it is being abused, and what safeguards are needed. Indigenous rights law experts might engage with this framing where they would not engage with a partisan-bias audit.

### Approach the Canadian Centre for Policy Alternatives or Parkland Institute
Both organizations publish policy research and have Alberta presence. Neither is a partisan actor, though both have a social-democratic orientation. A working paper through either would provide institutional standing without NDP capture risk. The CCPA's Track Changes election audit series (federal) is a potential model.

### Build the media prep kit before November 2026
A media package — one-page explainer, five-bullet summary, three key graphics, and a FAQ on common objections — prepared before November 2026 means that when the committee map drops and the 72-hour scorecard is produced, distribution can happen immediately. Journalists on deadline will not read a 30,000-word academic monograph. They need a one-page summary and a graphic they can publish.

### Pre-brief Duane Bratt and two other academics before the committee map
If Bratt and one or two other academics have read and understood the methodology before November 2026, they are positioned to comment for media on the scorecard immediately. Academic commentary in the first 24–48 hours of news coverage is what distinguishes "partisan attack" from "independent analysis of record." Scheduling those briefings now — six months out — gives time to address any methodological concerns they raise.

### Create a public-facing live dashboard for the November scorecard
A simple web dashboard showing the 17 tests with live pass/fail status as results are computed would be a genuinely compelling public communication tool. It would make the pre-registration concrete and visible to non-statisticians. A single GitHub Pages deployment — no hosting cost — could serve this purpose. It could link to the OSF pre-registration and to each test's methodology documentation.

### Approach the Globe and Mail's data journalism desk
The Globe's data team has covered redistricting before (federal electoral boundary reviews) and has the technical capacity to validate and amplify quantitative political analysis. A pitch to their data desk — before November, offering early access to the scorecard and methodology — is the highest-reach single media placement available.

## BLUE HAT — Strategic Process Synthesis

### What this pass has established

The White Hat mapped the strategic landscape: a pre-registered audit waiting for a committee map in a government-controlled political environment, with no documented uptake yet and several high-value relationship opportunities not yet realized.

The Red Hat surfaced the emotional stakes: excitement about the timing advantage, discomfort about the silence around the audit, concern about partisan capture, and anxiety about the solo-author sustainability question.

The Black Hat identified eight strategic risks, of which three are dominant: partisan capture, government indifference, and the possibility that the November committee map is clean (which would leave the retrospective findings as the primary record without prospective confirmation).

The Yellow Hat established that the counter-map (Issue #14), the Act amendment proposal, and the Enoch Cree Nation dimension are the most strategically leverageable under-developed assets. The 72-hour pre-registered scorecard is the project's greatest competitive advantage.

The Green Hat proposed seven specific strategic moves, anchored by the media prep kit and the academic briefing sequence as highest-priority before November.

The Alberta Electoral Boundary Audit is a methodologically rigorous, pre-registered, politically sensitive civic analysis sitting at the inflection point between academic contribution and public-interest impact, with five months before the moment that will determine whether it matters.

### What success looks like

Best case: The November 2026 scorecard produces findings consistent with the retrospective audit direction. It is published within 72 hours with pre-registered credibility. Two academics comment publicly. A Globe and Mail data story covers it. The Act amendment proposal enters legislative debate. The methodology paper is under review. An Indigenous rights scholar engages the Enoch Cree Nation finding. The Lunty committee's map faces substantive public scrutiny for the first time.

Minimum viable case: The scorecard is published within 72 hours, regardless of outcome, with full methodology visible. The pre-registration commitment is fulfilled. The project is part of the public record on this redistricting cycle. Future researchers can replicate everything.

### The verdict
The audit's civic value is not in doubt. Its public impact is entirely contingent on what happens in the next five months, and specifically on whether at least one credentialed academic, one journalist, and one affected community organization engage with it before November 2026. That engagement is achievable. It requires deliberate strategic action, not more methodology.
