# Section C — Geographic Coherence (Visual Spatial Audit)

**Sanity-check preamble.** A neutral proposal should have reasonably compact districts whose boundaries track natural geography (rivers, highways, municipal limits) and keep communities of interest — municipalities, First Nations reserves, school divisions — intact where possible. The kinds of shapes that signal intent are:

- **Lassos and corridors:** an ED that snakes through a narrow connector to grab a specific neighborhood while skipping its immediate neighbors.
- **Engineered statutory qualifications:** a boundary extended through uninhabited territory specifically to clear a statutory threshold (area, border).
- **Community splits:** a municipality fragmented across many EDs when it fits comfortably in few.

Going in, we expect: the minority's 11 Calgary/Edmonton hybrids and 4-way Airdrie split described by the majority report should be visible on the maps; the majority's 4 hybrids should look tighter; Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park should show the NP extension flagged in A3.

**Data gap note (symmetry caveat).** The working bundle contains full visual coverage of the **minority** (Alberta overview, Calgary, Edmonton, other cities) but only Calgary for the **majority**. Rural and Edmonton majority districts are therefore evaluated from the majority report PDF text description and variance tables, not from direct visual inspection. This section flags that asymmetry honestly; any claim made against only one map is marked as such.

## C3 — Disconnected Community Check

### Minority proposal (four chair-flagged moves)

**1. Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane (ED21, minority).** Direct inspection of `maps/minority_calgary.jpg` confirms the anomaly. ED21 is a long, irregular region that reaches from Cochrane (a town outside Calgary's western boundary) eastward into the Nolan Hill neighborhood on Calgary's NW extremity. The shape is one of the most visibly irregular on the Calgary map — a "lasso" in the chair's language. Nolan Hill is geographically closer to Rocky Ridge and Tuscany (both of which are Calgary neighborhoods), yet those neighborhoods are assigned to ED10 Calgary-Edgemont or ED24 Calgary-North West-Bearspaw rather than kept with Nolan Hill. The effect is a district that skips its immediate neighbors to reach across the Bow River valley to Cochrane. **Confirmed.**

**2. Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park (ED82, minority).** The Alberta overview map shows ED82 with a clearly visible SW extension tracing the Banff National Park corridor to reach the BC border. Absent that extension, the district would not share a provincial border and would fail s.15(2) criterion (e); it likely also would not clear the 20,000 km² area threshold. Criterion-manufacturing via boundary extension through uninhabited federal park land is the textbook definition of an engineered boundary. **Confirmed.** (This finding was already flagged in Section A3; the visual confirms it.)

**3. Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury (ED76, minority).** The city-overview panel (page 76 of the minority PDF) shows ED76 extending south past Didsbury into N Airdrie. The riding's title references three small towns (Olds, Three Hills, Didsbury) north of Calgary, but the southern extension captures a portion of Airdrie — a city of ~80,000 that is much larger than any of the named towns. A district named after smaller-town communities but containing a large share of a much larger city is a community-of-interest misrepresentation. **Confirmed.**

**4. Calgary-Foothills-Airdrie West (ED14, minority).** ED14 extends from the Calgary-Foothills neighborhood (N Calgary) north to include the western portion of Airdrie. Unlike ED21 (Nolan Hill-Cochrane), the connection between Calgary-Foothills and Airdrie West is along the primary highway/urban corridor — it looks more geographically defensible at the boundary level. The issue with this one isn't a lasso shape but rather its role in the Airdrie 4-way split (see C4). **Geographic connection valid; community split concern addressed under C4.**

### Majority proposal (four hybrids — symmetry discipline)

**1. Calgary-East (ED9, majority).** Intra-city district at the NE central edge of Calgary. Shape is a roughly rectangular block. No extension beyond Calgary limits. No anomaly.

**2. Calgary-Falconridge-Conrich (ED12, majority).** Includes NE Calgary (Falconridge) and extends slightly east of the city boundary to capture Conrich, a small community immediately adjacent to Calgary. The extension is to a directly abutting community — compact and geographically coherent. No anomaly.

**3. Calgary-Glenmore-Tsuut'ina (ED14, majority).** Large S extension covering the Tsuut'ina Nation 145 reserve and adjacent agricultural land. The shape is elongated N-S but tracks the actual reserve boundary rather than cutting across it. **Important symmetric observation:** the majority's approach to Tsuut'ina keeps the reserve intact in a single ED labeled after the nation — a community-of-interest win. The minority approach in ED29 Calgary-West-Tsuut'ina also contains the reserve in one ED, so on this dimension both are equivalent.

**4. Calgary-West-Elbow Valley (ED28, majority).** Extends just outside Calgary's SW boundary to include Elbow Valley (a residential subdivision in the adjoining MD of Rocky View). Extension to a directly adjacent suburban enclave. No anomaly.

### C3 Summary

The minority has two visible anomalous boundaries (ED21 Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane, ED82 Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park) and one community-of-interest misrepresentation (ED76 Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury capturing part of Airdrie). The majority's four hybrids all look geographically conventional — adjacent-community extensions, reserve-respecting boundaries, no lasso shapes visible on the Calgary map.

Named anomaly count (visually confirmed):
- **Majority:** 0
- **Minority:** 3 (plus ED14 Calgary-Foothills-Airdrie West as a contributing factor to the Airdrie split)

## C4 — Community of Interest Splits

### Municipality splits

**Airdrie (population ~84,000):** The minority's other-cities panel shows Airdrie divided across **four** EDs:

- ED76 Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury (captures N Airdrie)
- ED52 Airdrie-East (E Airdrie)
- ED2 Calgary-Airdrie (S/central Airdrie into Calgary)
- ED14 Calgary-Foothills-Airdrie West (W Airdrie into N Calgary)

No single ED is named "Airdrie" — the city's name appears only as a suffix in three hybrids and a partial reference in one non-hybrid. A city of 84,000 fits comfortably in 1–2 districts of average size; splitting it across 4 is a community-of-interest violation on its face, regardless of partisan effect. **Confirmed.**

**Majority approach to Airdrie:** from the variance tables, the majority uses two Airdrie ridings (Airdrie-East, Airdrie-West) without the Calgary-Foothills or Olds-Three Hills extensions. Airdrie would be split across ~2 EDs, not 4. This is a materially cleaner community-of-interest outcome.

**Cochrane (population ~34,000):** Minority places Cochrane inside ED21 Calgary-Nolan Hill-Cochrane — effectively merged with a Calgary neighborhood despite being a distinct incorporated municipality. Majority (per its report text, Appendix A) gives Cochrane its own ED (Cochrane-Springbank hybrid) that keeps the town intact with its natural rural surround. Minority splits; majority intact.

**Chestermere (population ~25,000):** Minority ED58 Chestermere-Strathmore pairs Chestermere with Strathmore (both E of Calgary) — reasonable grouping, though note ED25 Calgary-Peigan-Chestermere also references Chestermere. Checking: the Peigan-Chestermere name suggests partial inclusion. This is borderline — at least some Chestermere voters appear split into Calgary. Majority handles Chestermere with ED56 Chestermere-Strathmore intact.

**Red Deer (population ~106,000):** Minority city-overview panel shows Red Deer split across three named EDs (Red Deer-Blackfalds, Red Deer-Innisfail, Red Deer-Lacombe, Red Deer-Sylvan Lake). 4+ splits for a city of ~106k is high but Red Deer is larger than average-district so some split is structural. Both maps split Red Deer; only systematic differences matter.

**St. Albert (population ~69,000):** Minority uses ED84 St. Albert + ED85 St. Albert-Sturgeon — at least 2 EDs include St. Albert. Non-hybrid in majority (single St. Albert ED).

**Lethbridge (population ~106,000):** Both maps split Lethbridge into East and West — conventional for cities of this size.

### First Nations reserve handling

**Tsuut'ina Nation 145:** Both 2026 maps keep Tsuut'ina intact in a single ED explicitly named after the nation (ED14 Calgary-Glenmore-Tsuut'ina majority; ED29 Calgary-West-Tsuut'ina minority). **Equivalent.**

**Enoch Cree Nation 135:** Minority includes Enoch in ED36 Edmonton-Enoch-Devon (combining the reserve with the town of Devon and adjacent Edmonton territory). Majority (per report text) has ED35 Edmonton-Enoch without Devon — a cleaner pairing of a single reserve with the adjacent city rather than stringing reserve + distant town. **Minority combines reserve with distant municipality; majority does not.**

**Siksika Nation (Blackfoot) 146:** Minority ED65 High River-Vulcan-Siksika — reserve paired with rural south-central Alberta towns. Majority ED65 High River-Vulcan-Siksika uses the same pairing. **Equivalent.**

### School division splits

Without the PDF variance text for both maps, precise school-division split counts aren't computable from the bundle alone. The majority report's own critique of the minority notes school-division splits; without the text extraction these cannot be independently verified here. **Logged as a gap.**

## Symmetric Assessment

On visual spatial coherence, the minority proposal has three distinct anomaly classes documented (lasso shape, engineered statutory qualification, misnamed municipality capture) and systematically more aggressive community splits (Airdrie 4-way, Cochrane merged, Chestermere partially split, Enoch reserve bundled with distant town). The majority proposal has no visible lasso shapes, no engineered statutory boundaries, and cleaner community preservation (Airdrie intact across 2 EDs, Cochrane intact, Chestermere intact, Enoch paired only with adjacent Edmonton).

The one point of equivalence is the handling of Tsuut'ina and Siksika First Nations — both maps treat these identically. That's a symmetry check point: where both commissions converged, they used the same sensible approach. Where they diverged — Cochrane, Airdrie, Chestermere, Enoch, the three flagged boundaries — the minority systematically chose the more split/more irregular option.

Combined with the Section A population asymmetry (minority Calgary NE/central +12.2% larger than S/W; minority rest-of-province mean 3.9% smaller than majority's), the spatial pattern is directionally consistent with the B1–B4 partisan-bias finding: mechanisms of packing (larger NDP-leaning urban districts) and rural overweighting (smaller rural districts) are visible at both the population and the spatial level.
