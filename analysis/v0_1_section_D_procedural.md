# Section D — Procedural Audit

**Sanity-check preamble.** The cartographic question (Sections A, B, C) asks whether the maps themselves advantage one party. The procedural question asks whether the *process* that produced them — and the process that dispositioned them after tabling — met the fairness standards Canadian provinces have built up since *Reference re Provincial Electoral Boundaries (Saskatchewan)* [1991] 2 SCR 158. These are related but distinguishable: a fair process can produce flawed maps, and a flawed process can produce defensible maps. Treated in its own section.

## D1 — Independence of the drawing process

**The original commission.** Alberta's Electoral Boundaries Commission Act (ss. 3–5) establishes a five-member independent commission: the chair (appointed by the Chief Justice of Alberta), two government-nominated members, and two opposition-nominated members. The 2025–26 commission was constituted on this model and produced both the interim report (October 2025) and the final report (March 23, 2026), with the majority recommendation reflecting the chair plus the two opposition-nominated members and the minority recommendation reflecting the two government-nominated members.

This is materially identical to how every other Canadian province with a provincial boundary commission operates:

| Province        | Commission composition                                             | Appointment authority                            |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| British Columbia | 3 members, chair is a judge                                        | Judiciary + Lt-Gov in Council                   |
| Manitoba         | 3 members, chair is Chief Justice of Court of Queen's Bench       | Judiciary                                        |
| Ontario          | Follows federal Electoral Boundaries Readjustment Act process      | Federal appointment chain                        |
| Quebec           | Commission de la représentation électorale — 3 members, permanent | National Assembly and Chief Electoral Officer   |
| Saskatchewan     | 3 members, chair is Chief Justice                                  | Judiciary + Lt-Gov in Council                   |
| **Alberta**      | **5 members, chair nominated by Chief Justice**                    | **Judiciary + Lt-Gov in Council + gov/opp nominees** |

The Alberta model is actually slightly *more* pluralistic than neighbouring provinces (BC, Saskatchewan) because it adds explicit partisan-nominated members on both sides rather than relying on the judiciary + Lt-Gov chain alone. This is a strength of the original Act: disagreements are expected and institutionalized.

**The April 16, 2026 departure.** On April 16, 2026, the provincial government formally rejected the majority report and established an MLA committee chaired by Brandon Lunty (UCP) to oversee a new advisory panel, with a report-back date of November 2, 2026. An MLA committee with a government-caucus majority and a government-chair is a categorically different body from the independent commission. Under the standard Canadian framework:

- The commission's independence is what gives its output the legitimacy to bind the electoral map.
- A government-majority committee reviewing or overwriting the commission's output introduces partisan discretion at precisely the point where neutrality is most needed.
- The fact that a dissenting minority report exists does not, on its own, justify substituting the majority report with a government-drafted alternative. The dissent's purpose in the commission framework is to inform legislators and the public, not to displace the majority recommendation as the starting point for the legislature's review.

The April 16 action is not, by itself, a violation of the Act — section 15 of the Act allows the Legislative Assembly to amend the commission's proposals by ordinary legislation. But it does represent a departure from the *spirit* of the commission-based process, where the legislature accepts or lightly amends the recommendation rather than replacing the entire map.

## D2 — Public input alignment

**Baseline.** The commission received **1,140+ written submissions** during its public consultation phase in 2025. Submissions were published on the commission's website; public hearings were held in Calgary, Edmonton, Red Deer, Grande Prairie, Lethbridge, Medicine Hat, Fort McMurray, and Lloydminster.

**Majority report's claim.** The majority report (accepted by the chair and opposition-nominated members) states in Appendix C that the minority's hybrid configurations for **Airdrie, Cochrane, Chestermere, Red Deer, and St. Albert had no public support in the consultation record.** This is a checkable claim — the submissions are public — but independent verification requires downloading and text-searching the commission's submission archive, which is outside the token budget of this Phase 3 desk pass. The claim is on record in a document signed by the chair (an appointed judicial officer) and is subject to professional-responsibility norms. Treat as prima facie credible pending independent verification.

**Interpretation.** If accurate, it means the minority report introduces five hybrid configurations that were not proposed by, and are not supported by, any of the 1,140+ public submissions. The significance is that the standard defense of a disputed boundary is "the community asked for it"; that defense is unavailable for these five. The absence of public support does not by itself prove partisan intent, but it removes the most common non-partisan explanation for unusual boundary choices.

## D3 — Procedural fairness (interim → final)

**Interim report (October 2025).** The prompt describes the interim report as "unanimous" — all five commissioners signed the same recommendations. This is the commission's standard practice: the interim report is designed as a consultation document, and commissioners typically hold off partisan disagreement until after feedback.

**Final report (March 23, 2026).** Five months after the interim report, the commission split 3–2 with a majority and minority report. The majority largely extends the interim framework with adjustments responsive to the 1,140+ submissions; the minority departs from the interim framework in several specific ways — most notably the Airdrie 4-way split, the Nolan Hill-Cochrane hybrid, and the Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park extension documented in Sections A and C.

**The procedural-fairness question.** Did the minority report give the public fair notice of its departures before tabling? On a strict reading of the Act, the commission has no obligation to pre-publish minority positions — the public hearing process is between interim and final. But the departures from the interim framework that appear only in the minority (and not in the majority) were not tested in the public process. This means:

- The majority's ridings have been seen by the public, debated, and adjusted based on 1,140+ submissions.
- The minority's departures have not.

When the government on April 16 rejected the majority and proposed to work from a new advisory process, it was effectively promoting the less-publicly-vetted configuration over the more-publicly-vetted one. This is the procedural-fairness concern distinct from partisan-bias concern.

## D4 — Override mechanism comparisons

Instances where Canadian provincial governments have altered or rejected independent boundary commission reports:

**Quebec 1992 (Ryan–Parizeau redistribution).** The Commission de la représentation électorale's report in the early 1990s faced legislative amendment by the National Assembly before adoption. Amendments were limited in scope and did not replace the commission's map wholesale. Considered in retrospect as a stress test that the commission framework absorbed without structural damage.

**Ontario 1996 (Fewer Politicians Act).** The Harris PC government reduced Ontario's seat count from 130 to 103 by adopting federal (Chrétien-era) boundaries rather than running a provincial commission. This is a different class of action — adopting *another* independent commission's output, not substituting political judgment. The Supreme Court upheld the approach; it did not establish a precedent for rejecting a provincial commission's recommendation outright.

**British Columbia 2008 (Campbell Liberals, North).** The Campbell Liberal government initially rejected the Electoral Boundaries Commission's recommendation to consolidate Northern ridings and passed legislation retaining additional Northern seats. The issue was not partisan bias but a political commitment to Northern representation. The commission chair publicly opposed the override but did not seek judicial review.

**Alberta April 2026 (current matter).** Where does the April 16 action fall on this spectrum?

- It resembles BC 2008 in that the rejection is public and the government has stated its reasons.
- It departs from BC 2008 in that the rejected recommendation had no public-commitment conflict: the majority's map works from the same ±25% variance framework, the same s.15(2) rules, and the same public submissions as the minority's.
- It resembles no prior precedent in that the replacement process is not a second independent commission or a return to an earlier map, but a government-majority MLA committee overseeing a new advisory panel.

This last point is the material difference. BC 2008 did not substitute a new government-controlled process — it modified specific recommendations via ordinary legislation. Alberta April 2026 is, by the evidence available, the first instance in recent Canadian provincial practice of substituting a government-controlled drafting process for an independent commission's output mid-cycle. If the November 2, 2026 report-back produces a map materially different from the commission's majority recommendation, the process departure becomes a substantive one rather than merely procedural.

## Symmetric Assessment

On procedural grounds, the commission itself operated normally: five members, standard appointment, unanimous interim, divided final — exactly the process the Act contemplates. The three commissioners who signed the majority and the two who signed the minority both acted within their role.

The procedural-fairness concern is not about the commission's work. It is about the **government's April 16 response** to the commission's work:

- Rejecting the majority report *as a starting point* rather than working from it with legislative amendments is a departure from historical Canadian practice.
- Creating a government-majority MLA committee to oversee the replacement process is without recent provincial precedent.
- Promoting the less-publicly-vetted minority configuration over the more-publicly-vetted majority — given that the minority's five specific hybrid choices had no public support per the majority report's own account — is a procedural fairness concern.

This is distinct from the cartographic question. A government could override an independent commission with a legitimate-sounding alternative rationale (e.g., Northern representation in BC 2008) and that override would be procedurally concerning but not necessarily an attempt to manufacture partisan advantage. Here, because the minority's configurations align with the B1–B4 directional shift toward UCP (Section B prior work) and with the population asymmetry documented in Section A and the spatial anomalies in Section C, the procedural departure and the cartographic departure are plausibly related rather than independent. That connection is what distinguishes this from BC 2008 — and what makes the procedural question a substantive concern rather than just a process nit.
