Alberta Electoral Boundary Audit: Findings Brief

May 2026  ·  Official Elections Alberta shapefiles  ·  github.com/Ixby/alberta-electoral-boundaries-audit

What happened

Alberta has 89 seats in its legislature. An independent commission was appointed to draw the riding boundaries — the lines that determine which communities each seat represents. In 2025-26 the commission split: three members produced one map, two members produced a different one. On April 16, 2026, the government rejected both and gave the job to a five-MLA committee, three of whom are from the governing United Conservative Party (UCP). This audit compared the two commission maps using the same tests, applied equally to both.

Why riding boundaries matter: Move a line a few streets over and thousands of voters shift from one riding to another — changing who wins that riding without a single person changing their vote. Move enough lines and you can change the outcome of an election.

What is a "wasted vote"?

A vote is wasted if it didn't affect the outcome — either it went to a losing candidate, or it got piled onto a winner who already had more than enough. When one party's votes are wasted at a much higher rate, their supporters need more total votes to win the same number of seats.

Why does 5 seats matter?

You need 45 seats to control the legislature. At a tied vote, the majority map gives the NDP 48 seats — a governing majority. The minority map gives the NDP 43 — three short, and the UCP 46 — a majority for them instead. Same votes, different government.

What this does not say

It does not call either map illegal. The legal question in Canada is whether voters have "effective representation" under the Charter of Rights and Freedoms and whether the commission followed the Electoral Boundaries Commission Act. That is a question for courts and legislators — not this audit.

It does not say anyone cheated. Statistics show what a map does, not what its authors intended. The same outcome can come from deliberate choices, geography, or coincidence.

It accounts for geography. NDP voters are concentrated in city centres, which creates a built-in disadvantage on any neutral map. The 1 million random maps in the simulation use real Alberta geography — this is already factored in. The minority map is still extreme relative to those neutral draws.

How the analysis was done

All results use the official Elections Alberta riding maps (received May 6, 2026). Vote attribution is from the 2023 election, matched to each riding by geographic location. The 1 million random comparison maps were generated by a standard redistricting research tool (GerryChain) following the same population rules the commission had to follow. All tests were written down and publicly filed before results were examined — pre-registration IDs: OSF:6pt83, AsPredicted:#289,469, AsPredicted:#289,451.
