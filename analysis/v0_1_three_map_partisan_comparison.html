The Symmetric Test

Same four academic measures of partisan bias, applied to all three Alberta electoral map proposals on the table — the 2019 boundaries currently in force, the independent commission's majority recommendation, and the government appointees' minority recommendation.

2023 vote shares · 87/89 electoral divisions · Methodology: Stephanopoulos & McGhee (2014), McDonald & Best (2015)

Across all four canonical tests, the majority's plan preserves the partisan baseline of the existing 2019 boundaries within rounding error. The minority's plan moves the baseline 2 to 3 percentage points toward UCP — and 3 seats in the simulated 2023 outcome. The asymmetry between the two 2026 proposals is the measurable evidence. Neither map crosses the 7% efficiency-gap threshold flagged in US case law as presumptive gerrymandering, but the minority plan's directional consistency across four independent tests is the signal worth naming.

B2 — Efficiency gap

(NDP wasted votes − UCP wasted votes) / total. Negative = UCP wastes more (NDP-favorable map). Positive = NDP wastes more (UCP-favorable map). 7%+ flagged in US case law.

Shift from 2019: Majority +2.17 pp (mostly from going 87→89 EDs). Minority +2.94 pp toward UCP — 35% larger shift despite same ED count change.

B3 — Mean-median (NDP)

NDP mean two-party share across all districts vs the median district. Negative = NDP votes spread across many competitive districts (efficient). Toward zero = advantage erased.

Shift from 2019: Majority +0.07 pp (essentially unchanged — NDP keeps its distributional advantage). Minority +2.21 pp — wipes out the NDP advantage entirely.

B4 — NDP seats at 50/50 vote

Under uniform swing, if both parties received 50% of the province-wide two-party vote, how many seats would NDP win? In a perfectly symmetric map, both parties get 43-44 seats.

Shift from 2019: Majority +1 seat for NDP (slightly more competitive). Minority −3 seats for NDP — flips a 5-seat NDP advantage into a 3-seat UCP advantage.

Simulated 2023 outcome (NDP seats)

Apply 2023 vote shares directly to each map. The 2019 result is the actual 38-seat NDP outcome. The two 2026 plans are estimates based on best-fit 2019-to-2026 ED mapping.

Shift from 2019: Majority 0 (NDP wins 38 of 89). Minority −3 (NDP wins 35 of 89). In a province that just elected UCP 49–38, a 3-seat shift could be the difference between majority and minority in a closer race.

SECTION 04What the comparison establishes

The independent commission's majority recommendation does what a redistricting that preserves the partisan baseline looks like. Three of four metrics shift by less than 0.1 pp; the fourth (efficiency gap) shifts about 2 pp, but that shift is largely structural — adding 2 EDs to a map of 87 mathematically rebalances wasted vote counts.

The minority recommendation does something different. The same starting point, the same 2-ED expansion, the same vote shares — and four metrics that all move 2 to 3 pp in the same direction, costing NDP 3 seats in actual simulated outcome. The shift isn't an artifact of one test's quirks. It's the cumulative effect of four documented mechanisms: 11 Calgary hybrids that dilute urban NDP areas with rural UCP additions, packing of NE Calgary using the "electors not population" justification, an Edmonton inner-core merger, and three s.15(2) protected ridings that include the contested Rocky Mountain House-Banff Park boundary extension into uninhabited park land.

The government's choice last week was to reject the recommendation that preserves the baseline and create an MLA committee to oversee a new advisory panel. The minority plan — the one that shifts the baseline — is the one the panel is being set up to consider further. That sequencing is the procedural question, separate from but related to the cartographic one.

SECTION 05What this analysis is not

Methodology and limits

This is approximate. The 2019 boundaries are real and the analysis on them is exact — every district's vote totals come directly from Elections Alberta's official Statement of Vote. The two 2026 plans are estimates because ABEBC has not released proposed boundary shapefiles. Hybrid extensions are modeled by blending each urban core ED's 2023 vote with a rural baseline (33.5% NDP / 66.5% UCP, the average across non-NDP-stronghold rural Alberta). Splits and mergers handled by halving or summing the source EDs' votes.

The most rigorous test in the academic toolkit — Markov Chain Monte Carlo ensemble comparison via GerryChain (Python) or redist (R), available through MRU's data lab — generates 10,000+ alternative maps that meet the same legal criteria (population equality within ±25%, contiguity, compactness) and compares each proposal to the distribution. A proposal in the 95th percentile or beyond on partisan metrics is statistically inconsistent with neutral redistricting. That test requires the shapefiles. When they're released, run it.

Compactness scores (Polsby-Popper, Reock) and a precise community-of-interest split count also require shapefiles. The named "moves" the chair flagged by name — the Nolan Hill-Cochrane disconnect, the Banff Park extension, Airdrie split four ways, electors-not-population in NE Calgary — were addressed in the prior Calgary analysis at v0.1 of alberta_minority_gerrymander_visual.html.
