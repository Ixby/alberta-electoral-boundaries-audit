# Editor and Writer Critique — `report_public.md`

A staged debate between two personas over the Alberta Electoral Boundaries Audit feature as currently drafted. Marion is a senior magazine editor. Declan is a senior long-form writer. They disagree, concede, and end with a shared edit list.

---

## Round 1 — The Opening (Lede through Part I)

### Marion

The lede does something unusual and I want to defend it before I complain about it. Opening on the sentence Miller "would never write" — *"My majority colleagues do not agree with me on this point"* — is a genuinely editorial instinct. It gives us a document-as-scene, a judge alone on a page, and it sets up the Premier's April 16 citation as the counter-move. That is the piece's engine, and it's on page one. I'd sign off on the structural choice.

What I won't sign off on is the ratio of throat-clearing to argument between the lede and the primer. Look at paragraph five — the "Rachel Notley ... Jared Wesley ... Commissioner Greg Clark" stack. That is three voices doing the same work: naming the act and condemning it. Pick one. Clark's single line ("in Canada, we don't want elected officials drawing their own election maps") is the best of them and it's buried at the end. Promote Clark, cut Wesley to later in Part I where the process argument lives, save Notley for the moment you introduce the 2017 comparison. Right now it reads like a letters-to-the-editor page.

Second concern: the "three arguments" framing at line 33. You announce three, then in Part I you give us Nenshi-plus-Pancholi, Notley-plus-Wesley, and "the supermajority claim" attributed to Nenshi *and* Notley. Arguments one and three share a speaker. That's either a fourth argument hiding, or the tripartite structure is a rhetorical scaffold you're going to have to saw off in the kicker. I suspect the latter. Reader retention at the 30% mark depends on this frame holding; if it wobbles here, the scorecard in Part III has to work doubly hard.

Libel note: "engineered supermajority" and "gerrymander" are being attributed to named commissioners and named legislators. You're quoting them, which is the defensible move. Keep every one of those attributions clean, first-use, with date and venue. I'd bold-audit every adjective applied to Evans, Martin, Miller, Smith, and Lunty for the second read.

Concession: I called the lede 415 words long and I can hear myself being impatient. On a 5,200-word magazine feature for a literate audience, a 400-word lede that establishes scene, stakes, and three named speakers is defensible. I'd tighten, not cut.

### Declan

Marion, you want the Wesley-Notley-Clark stack thinned and I understand the instinct — three voices saying similar things in close succession *is* redundant on a line-by-line read. But here is what it's doing that a single voice can't. Clark is the inside man (a commissioner). Wesley is the expert witness (a scholar who chaired a ward commission). Notley is the former premier with standing to judge. The stack is a chord. Cut two of the notes and you have a single voice, which is an opinion. Leave all three and you have a consensus forming in real time. The reader at paragraph five is forming an impression of *who is saying this*, not *what is being said*. Keep the three. I would agree to tighten — you can lose twenty words out of that paragraph without losing a voice.

Where I do flag the opening is the sentence "I spent two days pulling population tables..." at line 23. That line is doing a necessary job — it grounds the audit as the writer's own work — but "two days" is a terrible piece of scale. It reads like a disclaimer: *don't expect too much.* Either cut the duration or own it harder. *"Two days, a laptop, and a municipal census"* would land. *"I spent two days"* sounds like an apology.

Second: the "Before the evidence, though, the arguments" closer to the lede, at line 27, is the kind of throat-clearing Marion was complaining about two paragraphs earlier. The lede should end on Miller alone on page 66. Instead it ends on a meta-sentence telling the reader what's coming. Trust the reader. Cut the transitional and let the Part I hed do the work.

Concession: Marion is right that the three-arguments frame has a speaker-overlap problem. I had not flagged it and I should have. Nenshi says two of the three. That's a frame problem. If the piece wants three arguments it needs three distinct speakers, or it needs to acknowledge in-line that the supermajority claim is Notley-originated and Nenshi-amplified. One sentence of provenance fixes it.

## Round 2 — The Evidence (Part II)

### Declan

This is where the piece either earns its magazine-feature price or it doesn't, and my verdict is: mostly earns. The "three scenes" gambit — the Airdrie woman with the advance-polling card, the two Calgarians ten minutes apart, the Icefields Parkway — is genuinely ambitious. Most audits never leave the spreadsheet. This one walks you into three kitchens. Scene one works because the card in her hand is a real object and the neighbour-by-neighbour enumeration is observational, not editorial. Scene three works because empty country is itself the argument — forty kilometres with nobody to represent is a photograph you can hold.

Scene two is the weakest of the three and Marion will not disagree. Two Calgarians who shop at the same Costco is a gesture toward scene-building, not a scene. We never get their names, we never get their kitchen, we never see them vote. The section then pivots to a table and the packing math, and the table is where the argument actually lives. My edit: either commit to scene two — give me one of them, with a name, walking to her car in the southwest UCP-leaning district — or drop the scene frame on section two entirely and let the table carry it. "Scene" announces a promise the prose does not keep.

Second concern, and this one I want Marion to push back on: the self-corrections. The Canmore-Banff reversal at line 25, the Rocky Mountain House threshold correction at line 140, the school-division partial retraction at line 266, the ensemble preliminary retraction at line 209. Each is individually defensible — this is what epistemic honesty looks like. Stacked, they risk a reader concluding the audit is unstable. The piece names three reversals in the lede ("Three of the findings contradicted the direction I expected") which is a virtue signal, but by the time the reader hits the fourth mid-body retraction the signal is inverting. I'd consolidate. Name the reversals once, at the top of Part II, in a short standing box. Don't strew them.

Concession: the four-tests / ensemble / hybrid-count / signatures stack at the end of Part II is doing a lot of formal work and I initially flagged it as too-many-tables-in-a-row. On re-read it's earned. Each table answers a different question. Keep the sequence.

### Marion

Declan, on scene two you and I agree, and I want to go further. The woman in scene one has a dental office and an eleven-year tenure and a polling card; those are three specific details doing work. Scene three has the Icefields Parkway and five named reserves and a road with nobody on it. Scene two has a Costco. That's a franchise, not a place. You're right that section two should either commit to the scene apparatus — pick one of the Calgarians, give her a living — or drop the scene frame and let the 12.2-percent gap be the lede of its own subsection. The mismatched commitment is what's reading wrong, not the underlying evidence.

On the self-corrections: I want to push back. Declan, you're measuring virtue-signal fatigue, and that's a real reader-response variable. But the alternative — burying reversals in a methods appendix — is worse. Alberta Views readers trust a piece more when the piece has already done the self-criticism for them. I'd keep every one of the reversals in-line. What I would change is the *rhetoric* around them. Line 25's "Three of the findings contradicted the direction I expected" is good. Line 140's "I was working from the wrong numbers" is also good — concrete, first-person, specific. But line 209's "an earlier preliminary pass had flagged them, and that preliminary result is retracted here" is bureaucratic prose sitting inside a magazine. That's an editor's fix, not a writer's. One pass to humanise the three body-level retractions and the stack stops reading as instability and starts reading as audit.

Structural complaint that matters more than either of ours so far: the commission's split section at line 77 buries the personnel. Miller is the only commissioner named in the lede. Clark appears three hundred words later. Evans, Martin, and Samson enter through a paragraph on line 81 that could be a Wikipedia infobox. Before you walk me through the map's disagreements, I need to know who the five people are — one sentence each, with who appointed them and what their public record signals. Currently, I cannot tell you without flipping back which way Evans and Martin are appointees. The split is 3-2 on almost every page of the audit and the reader needs to be able to keep score.

Concession: my first read had me sceptical of the minority-defences subsection at line 256 — I thought the arithmetic-defence rebuttal was doing too much with too little, rhetorically. On re-read, "Run the 2021 census numbers and every one of those defences fails" is exactly the blunt sentence the subsection needed. That lands. Keep it.

## Round 3 — Scorecard, Kicker, Endmatter (Part III through Source Trail)

### Marion

Part III is the piece's deliverable. Everything before it is evidence; Part III is the grading rubric. It has to do four things: (a) state the rubric unambiguously, (b) pre-commit the grader to it, (c) show where the minority map sits today, and (d) tell the reader what to watch for on November 2. It does three and a half of those.

The half it doesn't quite do is the pre-commitment. Line 302 says the checklist was "pre-registered in the audit's technical report before the committee convened." Good. I want the link in that sentence, not in the end-matter. A pre-registration claim without a timestamped link is the kind of thing hostile readers will mock in a letter. Give me the commit hash or the paragraph number.

Second structural issue: the scorecard's four tiers — strong, weak, process, look-bad-but-not-sure-signs — are the piece's original contribution and I like them. What I don't like is that "strong signals" runs to seven bullet points of prose and "sure-sign gerrymander" is a pull-quote paragraph after the tiers. The pull quote ought to be the section's sub-head, not a coda. Move it up. Let the reader hit the rubric first and the tiers below it as evidence of how you'd score.

Third: the kicker does something I respect and something I'd fight over. The respect: opening on Calgary-Acadia's five-hundredths-of-a-percent margin is the best rhetorical move in the piece — a real number, a real race, a stake the reader can hold. The fight: the kicker then argues itself out of urgency. "The 2023 election was not close enough ... UCP won 49 to NDP's 38 ... 338Canada snapshot projects UCP 63 to NDP 24." You walked the reader up a hill and then told them the view is fine. That's intellectually honest and rhetorically deflating. Either cut the 338Canada paragraph or pair it with the ten-point-swing caveat in the same paragraph, so the reader doesn't spend thirty seconds thinking the piece has folded.

Libel flag on Part III: "publicly-supported configurations (Rocky Mountain House, Olds-Three Hills-Didsbury, Chestermere) get dropped while unsupported ones (the Airdrie four-way, the Nolan Hill-Cochrane corridor) get kept" is a factual claim about a map the Lunty committee has not yet drawn. It's framed conditionally ("If the committee preserves...") which is fine. But keep the conditional framing tight through every adjacent sentence. One slip into indicative voice and the magazine is exposed.

Concession: I flagged the "Your Turn" civic-action section on first read as the sort of thing a long feature shouldn't do — it breaks the fourth wall. I changed my mind. The repository call-out ("Share the scripts, not the op-eds") is a piece of rhetorical machinery the audit has earned. It turns the reader from spectator to grader, which is the piece's whole thesis. Keep it.

### Declan

Marion, on the scorecard's pull quote — agreed. "A sure-sign gerrymander in November looks like..." is the best sentence in Part III and it belongs at the top of the section, not the bottom. That is a straightforward fix.

Where I push back is the kicker. You want the 338Canada paragraph cut or caveated. I want it kept, exactly where it is, because it is the piece's most important self-discipline. A gerrymander-audit that tells you *yes, the map is partisan, and also it won't change the 2027 outcome* is doing something almost no other piece of partisan-map writing does. It refuses the easy climax. The reader who reaches "In a 2023-style blowout, it does not change who governs" is a reader who trusts the author. Cut that and the piece becomes advocacy with a rubric bolted on. Keep it and the piece is an audit. I'd fight to keep the paragraph and I'd fight to keep its flatness.

What I would change is one line in the kicker. "Ask the 2023 NDP candidate in Calgary-Acadia about five one-hundredths of a percentage point." That's a rhetorical move, and it's half-landing. Give me the candidate's name. Diana Batten, if I'm remembering right. Name her. "Ask Diana Batten" has a pulse. "Ask the 2023 NDP candidate" is a stock phrase. Small fix, outsized payoff.

On the endmatter: "The Limits" at line 364 is a small gem. Four paragraphs, each one a specific concession, each one the kind of thing a careful reader was going to raise in their own head. It inoculates the piece against its worst critics. Move nothing. I would only suggest it appears before "The Method" rather than after — a reader coming out of the kicker wants the honest limits before the methods footnote, because the limits are where the piece's judgement lives and the method is where its machinery does.

Concession: I was sceptical of "Your Turn" on a structural basis — feature journalism traditionally ends, it doesn't instruct. Marion defended it on rhetorical grounds and she's right. The section works because the piece has built a rubric the reader can actually use. Without Part III's scorecard, "Your Turn" would be empty exhortation. With it, it's a tool. Keep.

## What We Agreed On

Seven edits we would both sign off on, in priority order. These are the changes that matter; everything else is trim.

1. **Split the lede's voice-stack with provenance.** The Notley/Wesley/Clark paragraph at line 19 keeps all three voices (Declan won this), but tighten for twenty words and make Clark's line the stack's climax, not its middle. In the "three arguments" frame at line 33, add one sentence of provenance noting that the supermajority claim is Notley-originated and Nenshi-amplified — resolving the speaker-overlap Marion flagged.

2. **Fix scene two or drop the scene frame.** The "two Calgarians" passage at line 123 has no kitchen, no name, no specificity; it reads as apparatus, not scene. Either commit — give us one Calgarian with a name, a neighbourhood, an object in her hand — or drop "Scene two" as a hed and let the packing table carry the subsection as its own argument.

3. **Restructure the commissioners' introduction.** Line 81 currently requires the reader to flip back to keep score on the 3-2 split. Give each of the five commissioners one sentence of identity — name, nominator, public record — in a short roster paragraph at the top of "The commission's split" before any map disagreement is named.

4. **Promote the pull quote in Part III.** "A sure-sign gerrymander in November looks like..." at line 312 is the section's thesis and belongs above the four-tier checklist, not below it. Move it to the section's opening and let the tiers read as its evidence.

5. **Humanise the body-level retractions.** The reversals at line 140 (Rocky Mountain House) and line 266 (school divisions) are already in first-person and they work. Line 209's ensemble-retraction ("an earlier preliminary pass had flagged them, and that preliminary result is retracted here") is bureaucratic; rewrite in the voice of the other two. Don't consolidate into a standing box — Marion won that argument; retractions belong where the reader encounters the finding.

6. **Link the pre-registration claim.** Line 302's assertion that the rubric was pre-registered before the committee convened needs a timestamped citation in the sentence itself — a commit hash, a paragraph reference to the technical report, or a dated URL. Without it, the pre-commitment rhetoric is a vulnerability.

7. **Name Diana Batten in the kicker.** Line 330's "Ask the 2023 NDP candidate in Calgary-Acadia" is generic where it could be specific. Naming the candidate gives the kicker's rhetorical move a pulse — and costs nothing.

Both personas would leave the kicker's 338Canada paragraph intact, leave "Your Turn" and "The Limits" substantially alone, and leave the lede's opening on Miller's page-66 sentence exactly where it is. The piece earns its length. It needs seven honest cuts and one promotion, not a rewrite.
